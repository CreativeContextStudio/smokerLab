**Asado de tira** is the soul of the Uruguayan parrilla. These thin cross-cut short ribs cook low and slow over a full ember bed \u2014 bone-side down, patient, unhurried \u2014 until the fat renders completely and the surface turns a deep, crackling mahogany. For 4\u20136 people, plan 3\u20134 lb of tira and 60\u201390 minutes of steady heat.

***

## Fuego / Fire Setup

Start your fire **60\u201390 minutes before you intend to cook.** The parrilla runs on embers, never on flame.

1. Build a wood stack in the **brasero** (the ember-making pit beside or beneath the grate) using 3\u20134 hardwood logs (quebracho, eucalyptus, or oak). Cross-stack them for airflow.
2. Light with newspaper or a natural fire starter \u2014 never lighter fluid.
3. Let the logs burn fully down to glowing orange coals. This takes 45\u201360 minutes. The fire should look like a dense, luminous orange bed, not a roaring flame.
4. **Rake a generous layer of embers** under the full grate using your shovel. You want an even carpet of coals across the entire cooking zone.
5. **Set the grate HIGH** \u2014 15\u201320 cm (6\u20138 inches) above the embers. Asado de tira is a slow cook. High heat will burn the fat before it renders.

**Target ember color:** White-gray on the surface with glowing orange underneath. If the embers still look flame-red and active, wait 10 more minutes.

**Do\u2019s**
- Keep the brasero burning throughout the cook \u2014 rake fresh embers under the grate every 20\u201330 minutes to maintain steady heat.
- Let the embers settle to an even glow before placing meat.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t cook over flame. If you see orange licks under the grate, raise the grate or wait.
- Don\u2019t rush the ember-making phase. Green embers give uneven, bitter heat.

***

## Preparaci\u00F3n / Meat Prep

1. Remove the tira from refrigeration **30\u201345 minutes** before it goes on the grate. Cold meat hitting hot metal steams instead of sears.
2. **Season the bone side generously** with sal gruesa (coarse sea salt) \u2014 press it in with your hand so it sticks. Season the meat side just before placing on the grate.
3. No oil, no rub, no marinade. The fat cap and the sal gruesa are all you need.

**Do\u2019s**
- Season the bone side first \u2014 this is the side going down first, and the salt will begin drawing moisture as the meat comes to temperature.
- Pat the meat side dry with a paper towel if it looks wet.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t marinate asado de tira. Acid (lemon, vinegar) will begin to denature the proteins before the fire can do its work.
- Don\u2019t trim the fat cap \u2014 that slow-rendering fat is the entire point.

***

## Secuencia de Parrilla / Grill Sequence

### Phase 1: Bone-side down, slow rendering (40\u201360 min)

1. Place the tira **bone-side down** on the high grate over the even ember bed.
2. Close your ears to your guests. This phase is slow and quiet. Leave it alone.
3. After 30 minutes, listen: you should hear a steady, gentle **sizzle** as the fat renders and drips onto the embers. If you hear nothing, rake fresh coals underneath. If you hear violent popping and see flare-ups, raise the grate.
4. Check the fat at 40 minutes: it should be translucent and pulling away from the bone slightly. The bone side should be deeply browned, almost mahogany.

**Sensory cues for Phase 1:**
- Steady low sizzle: good
- Fat rendering and dripping visibly: good
- Bone beginning to pull away from the ends of the ribs: 10\u201315 more minutes, almost done with this side

### Phase 2: Flip and finish (20\u201330 min)

1. **Flip the tira** meat-side down. Salt the bone side now that it\u2019s facing up.
2. Lower the grate by one notch if you want a bit more crust. The fat has already rendered \u2014 you\u2019re just finishing the meat side now.
3. Cook 20\u201330 more minutes. The meat will tighten and the surface will develop a golden, slightly crisp crust.

**Sensory cues for Phase 2:**
- Surface color: deep brown with golden patches where the fat pooled
- Press test: the meat should feel firm but not rock-hard \u2014 medium with a slight give
- Carryover: pull at medium (the tira is not eaten rare; the long cook is intentional)

### Phase 3: Rest

- Pull off the grate and rest **5\u201310 minutes** on a wooden board.
- Cut between the bones with a heavy knife. Serve immediately.

***

## Servicio y Acompa\u00F1amientos / Serving and Sides

- **Serve with:** chimichurri uruguayo and salsa criolla passed at the table \u2014 never applied during cooking.
- **Classic accompaniments:** ensalada mixta (lettuce, tomato, onion), papas al rescoldo (potatoes from the embers), crusty bread.
- **Drink:** A glass of Tannat \u2014 Uruguay\u2019s deep, tannic red \u2014 is the natural companion.
- **Sequence note:** Asado de tira arrives after the achuras (morcilla, chorizo) but before the faster cuts like entra\u00F1a.

**Cultural note:** Asado de tira is a test of an asador\u2019s patience. The long, low cook is not a mistake or a limitation \u2014 it is the technique. The unhurried pace is what separates an asado from simply \u201cgrilling.\u201d

***

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Start the fire 60\u201390 minutes before cooking \u2014 no shortcuts.
- Use high grate position and steady, low ember heat.
- Season with sal gruesa only \u2014 bone side first, meat side just before placing.
- Let the fat render completely before flipping.
- Keep the brasero fed with fresh wood throughout the cook.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t cook over active flame \u2014 embers only.
- Don\u2019t rush by lowering the grate early \u2014 unrendered fat will flare and burn.
- Don\u2019t marinate or use sauces during cooking.
- Don\u2019t walk away without tending the brasero \u2014 dying embers mean uneven heat.
- Don\u2019t slice until it has rested.
