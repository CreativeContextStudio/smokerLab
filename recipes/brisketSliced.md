Here’s a **tight, step‑by‑step guide** for **3–5 lb sliced brisket** on your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S offset**, focused on juicy, sliceable texture with good bark and clean smoke. Times are for 250–275°F; always use **probe tenderness**, not just the clock, to decide when to pull.  

***

## 1. Choose and prep the brisket

- **Target:** 3–5 lb brisket flat or small packer, with at least some fat cap (1/8–1/4").  
- **Trim:**  
  - Keep a thin fat cap; trim big ragged pieces and any papery skin, but leave interior fat marbling.  

**Do’s**  
- Trim and pat dry before seasoning.  
- Apply a **salt‑heavy rub** (e.g., 2:1 salt/pepper, garlic/onion powder, pinch of paprika) 30–60 minutes before fire‑up. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t over‑trim; you need fat for moisture and bark formation.  
- Don’t saturate the rub with sugar if you’re smoking low‑and‑slow.  

***

## 2. Set up the Royal Gourmet smoker

- **Target temp:** 250–275°F for a 3–5 lb piece.  
- **Fire:**  
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox, building a **minion‑style fire** (2/3 unlit, 1/3 lit) with 1–2 small wood chunks (hickory, oak, pecan). [best practice]  
- **Vents:**  
  - Stack **mostly open**; firebox intake **1/4–1/3 open** to hold 250–275°F. [best practice]  
- **Pan:**  
  - Place a shallow water pan on the grate on the **stack side** to buffer temps. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Double‑check that **door gaskets and seams are sealed**. [best practice]  
- Use **baffle / tuning plates** so the heat travels smoothly from firebox to stack side. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t open the lid during the warm‑up phase.  
- Don’t crank the firebox vent wide open and the stack closed; that creates chaos.  

***

## 3. Place the brisket on the offset

- **Position:** Stack side of the chamber, not next to the firebox.  
- **Orientation:**  
  - Put the **thicker end toward the firebox side** if there’s a big difference in thickness.  
  - Fat‑side up or down is fine; fat‑side up gives a bit more forgiveness and better bark. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Insert a **meat‑probe thermometer** into the thickest part, avoiding big fat pockets.  
- Insert a **grate‑level probe** near the meat to track smoker temp. [best practice]  
- Close the lid and **do not open it for 2 hours** to let the bark form. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t put the brisket on the hottest side of the grill.  
- Don’t move it around during the first part of the cook.  

***

## 4. Smoke naked to build bark (2–4 hours)

- **Goal:** Dark, tacky, rub‑locked bark; internal temp ~170–180°F.  
- **Spritz:**  
  - After 2 hours, open the lid **quickly** and spritz the surface with beef broth, water, or a 50/50 mix. Repeat every 30–45 minutes if the surface looks dry. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Let the bark set before you think about wrapping; for a sliced brisket, you usually **keep it naked the whole time**. [best practice]  
- Probe at 160–170°F, then 180°F, checking tenderness.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t wrap too early; that softens the bark and makes it hard to slice cleanly.  
- Don’t keep the lid open for long photos or unnecessary checks.  

***

## 5. Time and temp for 3–5 lb sliced brisket at 250–275°F

**Goal:** 195–205°F internal, **probe‑tender** but still sliceable (not shredded). [best practice]  

| Situation | Time |
| --- | --- |
| Naked only (recommended for slicing) | 4–6 hours total (3–5 lb at 250–275°F). |  

**Approximate milestones:**

- After 2–3 hours: Check bark and temp; it should be dark and set at ~160–175°F.  
- After 3–4 hours: It should be 180–190°F and firm.  
- After 4–5 hours: Start probing for tenderness; at 195–200°F, it should slide in like softened butter.  

**Do’s**  
- Use **probe tenderness**, not just temp, as your cutoff. [best practice]  
- If you see it pushing past 205°F and getting mushy‑soft, pull it sooner.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t force it past 205°F; you’ll lose slicing texture.  
- Don’t wrap unless you’re dealing with weather or a very lean piece.  

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (3–5 lb sliced brisket)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your brisket’s behavior.

| Time since fire‑up | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Fire up the CC1830S; build a minion‑fire, stabilize at 250–275°F. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | Load the brisket fat‑side up on the stack side, close the lid. [best practice] |
| 2 hours | Quick spritz; check bark. If it’s still light, keep it naked and spritz again in 30–45 minutes. [best practice] |
| 2–3 hours | Continue smoking; no opening unless you need to check bark or temp. |
| 3–4 hours | Check internal temp; it should be 180–190°F. Keep smoking. |
| 4–5.5 hours | Start probing every 30–45 minutes once above 190°F. Aim for 195–205°F and probe‑tender. [best practice] |

**Target finish:** 195–205°F internal, **probe‑tender** but still sliceable.  

***

## 7. Resting and slicing

**Do’s**  
- Pull when it’s **195–205°F and probe‑tender**, then keep it **wrapped in foil or butcher paper** and rest **at least 1.5–2 hours** on the counter. [best practice]  
- After 1.5–2 hours, unwrap carefully and let it sit 15–30 minutes to firm up.  
- Slice **against the grain** in 1/4–1/2 inch thick slices; thicker slices hold more juice and keep that brisket texture. [best practice]  
- Serve warm or at room temperature; sliced brisket holds very well.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t slice it immediately off the smoker; it will be tough and dry.  
- Don’t slice too thin; you’ll lose the juicy, meaty bite.  
- Don’t discard the juices; skim off any fat and use them for dipping or drizzling.  

***

## 8. Do’s / Don’ts for sliced brisket on your offset

**Do’s**  
- Use a moderate rub and let it sit 30–60 minutes before lighting.  
- Keep the smoker sealed, use baffle/tuning plates, and manage temps via the firebox intake. [best practice]  
- Keep it mostly **naked**, unless you’re worried about drying out, to maximize bark and slicing texture.  
- Use a **thermometer** and **probe tenderness** as your final decision‑maker. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t over‑smoke; keep wood chunks modest and stop active smoke once the bark is set.  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check on the brisket.  
- Don’t skip the rest; it’s critical for juicy, sliceable meat.
