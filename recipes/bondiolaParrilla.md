**Bondiola** \u2014 the pork shoulder-neck \u2014 is the most forgiving and rewarding cut for a long asado. Marbled with fat throughout, it goes on the parrilla early, over a low, patient ember bed, and comes off 90\u2013120 minutes later impossibly tender and juicy with a deep golden crust. For 6\u20138 guests, plan a 3\u20135 lb piece and put it on the grate right after the chorizo and morcilla are served.

***

## Fuego / Fire Setup

Bondiola needs the same steady, patient approach as asado de tira \u2014 low heat, high grate, sustained over a long cook.

1. Start your fire **60\u201390 minutes** before cooking. Build 3\u20134 hardwood logs in the brasero and let them burn to a full, deep ember bed.
2. Rake coals under the full grate area.
3. **Set the grate HIGH** \u2014 15\u201320 cm (6\u20138 inches) above the embers. Bondiola is a big, dense cut. Too much heat will char the surface while the inside stays raw.
4. Hold your hand at grate height: you should feel steady warmth but be comfortable there for 5\u20136 seconds. That\u2019s the right level.
5. Maintain the brasero throughout \u2014 one fresh log every 25\u201330 minutes and fresh ember raking as needed.

**Do\u2019s**
- Think of bondiola as the pork equivalent of asado de tira: it rewards low, consistent heat and time.
- Plan for 90\u2013120 minutes on the grate \u2014 put it on early in the asado, right after the achuras are served.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t rush with a low grate and high heat. Pork fat at high heat flares, and bondiola has a lot of fat.
- Don\u2019t let the brasero go cold halfway through \u2014 an uneven cook produces a dry exterior with an underdone center.

***

## Preparaci\u00F3n / Meat Prep

1. Remove bondiola from refrigeration **30\u201345 minutes** before cooking.
2. Season all sides generously with **sal gruesa**. Press it in firmly.
3. Optional: a light dusting of dried oregano and black pepper adds a classic touch.
4. No oil, no marinade \u2014 bondiola has enough internal fat to self-baste through the entire cook.

**Do\u2019s**
- If the piece is very thick (over 8 cm), consider scoring the fat cap lightly in a crosshatch pattern to help rendering.
- Season generously \u2014 the salt crust on the exterior is part of the flavour.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t trim the fat. It is the entire reason bondiola is this good.
- Don\u2019t marinate. This cut doesn\u2019t need it.

***

## Secuencia de Parrilla / Grill Sequence

### Phase 1: All-sides browning (20\u201330 min)

1. Place bondiola on the high grate, fat-side down first.
2. After 10\u201315 minutes, when the fat side is golden and starting to render, rotate to the next side.
3. Brown all four main sides over the first 20\u201330 minutes \u2014 rotating every 8\u201310 minutes.
4. The goal is a uniform golden-brown crust on all sides before the main slow cook.

### Phase 2: The long slow cook (60\u201390 min)

1. Once all sides are browned, settle the bondiola into a position and let it cook slowly, turning every 20\u201325 minutes.
2. The smell will shift from searing meat to something deeper and more caramelised \u2014 that\u2019s the connective tissue and fat breaking down.
3. At the **75-minute mark**, press the thickest part with tongs. A piece that is done has significant give and feels soft when pressed firmly. An underdone piece feels firm and resistant.

**Sensory cues for doneness:**
- Exterior: deep golden-brown, almost mahogany in places
- Fat: rendered and golden-translucent
- Press test: the meat gives generously under firm pressure \u2014 almost pillowy
- Internal temperature (optional): **70\u201375\u00B0C (160\u2013165\u00B0F)** at the thickest point

### Phase 3: Rest

- Pull off the grate and rest **10 minutes** on a board.
- Slice against the grain into thick rounds or pull apart into chunks for serving.

***

## Servicio y Acompa\u00F1amientos / Serving and Sides

- **Slice thickly** and arrange on a board \u2014 bondiola does not need to be neat. Rustic is right.
- **Chimichurri** at the table.
- **Salsa criolla** for brightness.
- **Classic sides:** ensalada mixta, papas al rescoldo, morrones asados.
- **Drink:** Tannat holds its own against the richness of bondiola. A glass of young Tannat alongside is the classic Uruguayan pairing.

**Cultural note:** Bondiola has a devoted following among Uruguayan asadores precisely because it is humble \u2014 a shoulder cut that many overlook in favour of expensive beef. But cooked properly on a parrilla with patience and a full ember bed, it is extraordinary. On a Sunday asado where some guests are vegetarian-adjacent or avoiding beef, bondiola is the host\u2019s gracious solution.

***

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Put bondiola on early \u2014 it needs 90\u2013120 minutes.
- High grate, steady low heat the whole way.
- Brown all sides before settling into the slow phase.
- Rotate every 20\u201325 minutes to ensure even cooking.
- Rest before slicing.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t rush with high heat \u2014 the fat will flare and the exterior will burn before the inside cooks.
- Don\u2019t trim the fat.
- Don\u2019t forget to maintain the brasero \u2014 90 minutes is a long time and the fire needs tending.
- Don\u2019t slice before resting \u2014 the juices are still moving.
