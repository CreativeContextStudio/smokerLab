Perfect—here’s a **tight, all‑inclusive script** for **smoking your own cure‑it‑yourself pastrami (3–5 lb brisket flat)** on the **Royal Gourmet CC1830S** at **250–275°F**, with **medium‑to‑thick slices** in mind. This assumes you have refrigeration space and can plan 7–10 days ahead.  

***

## 1. Timeline overview

- **Days –7 to –1:** Cure and dry the brisket.  
- **Day 0:** Smoke, rest, slice.  

We’ll walk through each phase with concrete times and targets.

***

## 2. Cure the brisket (Days –7 to –1)

### Brine ingredients (per 1 lb brisket; scale as needed)

- 50 g kosher salt per quart  
- 30 g sugar per quart  
- 1 tsp pink curing salt (optional but classic)  
- 1 tbsp crushed black peppercorns per lb brisket  
- 1 tbsp crushed coriander seeds per lb brisket  
- 1 tbsp crushed yellow mustard seeds per lb brisket  
- 1 tbsp crushed juniper berries per lb brisket  
- 1 tsp garlic powder per lb brisket  
- 1 tsp smoked paprika per lb brisket  

### Instructions

- **Day –7:**  
  - Dissolve salts and sugar in water, then add remaining spices. Cool to room temp.  
  - Submerge the **3–5 lb brisket flat** in the brine.  
  - Refrigerate. This is your **Day 0 of the cure**.  

- **Days –6 to –1:**  
  - Flip the brisket in the brine once daily.  
  - Keep it refrigerated.  

- **Day –1 (or very early on Day 0):**  
  - Remove brisket, rinse well under cold water to remove excess salt.  
  - Pat extremely dry with paper towels.  
  - Place on a wire rack over a tray, uncovered, in the fridge for **12–24 hours** to form a pellicle. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Maintain cold temps (≤40°F) during the entire cure.  
- Use a **plastic bag or container** to keep the brine contained.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t shorten the cure under 5–7 days; you’ll miss the classic pastrami flavor.  
- Don’t let the brisket sit at room temp for long during the brine or rinse.  

***

## 3. Make the pastrami rub (Day 0, morning)

### Rub ingredients

- 2 tbsp black pepper, coarsely ground  
- 2 tbsp coriander, ground  
- 1 tbsp yellow mustard seeds, crushed  
- 1 tbsp juniper berries, crushed  
- 1 tsp garlic powder  
- 1 tsp smoked paprika  
- 1 tsp brown sugar (optional, for a hint of sweetness)  

### Instructions

- Mix the rub well.  
- Take the brisket out of the fridge, rub **generously** on all sides, especially the fat cap and edges.  
- Let it sit uncovered at room temp for **30–60 minutes** so the spices adhere and the surface dries slightly. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Use a mortar and pestle or spice grinder for whole spices; fresh‑cracked adds flavor.  
- Rub until the surface looks completely coated.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t apply a wet glaze; keep it dry‑spiced for that pastrami bark.  
- Don’t skip the 30–60 min rest before lighting the fire.  

***

## 4. Set up the Royal Gourmet smoker (Day 0, fire‑up)

- **Target temp:** 250–275°F for a 3–5 lb brisket flat.  
- **Fire:**  
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox, building a **minion‑style fire** (2/3 unlit, 1/3 lit) with 1–2 small mild wood chunks (apple, hickory, or maple). [best practice]  
- **Vents:**  
  - Stack **mostly open**; firebox intake **1/4–1/3 open** to hold 250–275°F. [best practice]  
- **Pan:**  
  - Place a shallow water pan on the grate on the **stack side** to buffer temps. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Double‑check that **door gaskets and seams are sealed**. [best practice]  
- Use **baffle / tuning plates** so the heat travels smoothly from firebox to stack side. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t open the lid during the warm‑up phase.  
- Don’t crank the firebox vent wide open and the stack closed; that creates chaos.  

***

## 5. Place the brisket flat on the offset

- **Position:** Stack side of the chamber, not next to the firebox.  
- **Orientation:**  
  - Put the **fat cap up** for a nice, shiny, glossy bark.  
  - You can place a **thin sheet of water** underneath the brisket, but it’s not required.  

**Do’s**  
- Insert a **meat‑probe thermometer** into the thickest part, avoiding big fat pockets.  
- Insert a **grate‑level probe** near the meat to track smoker temp. [best practice]  
- Close the lid and **do not open it for 2 hours** to let the bark form. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t put the brisket on the hottest side of the grill.  
- Don’t move it around during the first part of the cook.  

***

## 6. Smoke time and temp for 3–5 lb smoked pastrami at 250–275°F

**Goal:** 195–205°F internal, **probe‑tender** (slides in like softened butter), with a **dark, glossy, pastrami‑style bark**. [best practice]  

| Situation | Time |
| --- | --- |
| Naked only (pastrami‑style) | 4–6 hours total (3–5 lb at 250–275°F). |  

**Approximate milestones:**

- After 2–3 hours: Check bark; it should be **dark, glossy, and set** (pastrami color).  
- After 3–4 hours: Internal temp ~180–190°F; keep smoking.  
- After 4–5.5 hours: Probe every 30–45 minutes once above 190°F; aim for 195–205°F and probe‑tender. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Use **probe tenderness**, not just temp, as your cutoff. [best practice]  
- If you see it pushing past 205°F and getting mushy, pull it sooner.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t wrap unless you’re dealing with bad weather or a very lean piece.  
- Don’t skip the water pan; it helps keep the meat moist.  

***

## 7. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (3–5 lb smoked pastrami)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your brisket’s behavior.

| Time since fire‑up | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Fire up the CC1830S; build a minion‑fire, stabilize at 250–275°F. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | Load the brisket flat fat‑side up on the stack side, close the lid. [best practice] |
| 2 hours | Quick spritz; check bark. If it’s still light, keep it naked and spritz again in 30–45 minutes. [best practice] |
| 2–3 hours | Continue smoking; no opening unless you need to check bark or temp. |
| 3–4 hours | Check internal temp; it should be 180–190°F. Keep smoking. |
| 4–5.5 hours | Start probing every 30–45 minutes once above 190°F. Aim for 195–205°F and probe‑tender. [best practice] |

**Target finish:** 195–205°F internal, **probe‑tender** but still sliceable.  

***

## 8. Resting and slicing (medium‑to‑thick slices)

**Do’s**  
- Pull when it’s **195–205°F and probe‑tender**, then keep it **wrapped in foil or butcher paper** and rest **at least 1.5–2 hours** on the counter. [best practice]  
- After 1.5–2 hours, unwrap carefully and let it sit 15–30 minutes to firm up slightly.  
- Slice **against the grain** in **1/4–1/3 inch thick slices** for that **medium‑to‑thick, deli‑style pastrami**. [best practice]  
- Serve with mustard, rye bread, or a light pickle on the side for authenticity.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t slice too thin like a pulled brisket; you want some meaty bite.  
- Don’t skip the rest; it’s critical for juicy, sliceable meat.  
- Don’t discard the juices; they hold great flavor and can be used for dipping.  

***

## 9. Do’s / Don’ts for smoked pastrami on your offset

**Do’s**  
- Use a **fresh brisket flat** and cure it properly for at least 5–7 days.  
- Keep the smoker sealed, use baffle/tuning plates, and manage temps via the firebox intake. [best practice]  
- Use a **thermometer** and **probe tenderness** as your final decision‑maker. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t over‑smoke; keep wood chunks modest and stop active smoke once the bark is set.  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check on the brisket.  
- Don’t skip the cure; it’s what gives pastrami its unique flavor and texture.
