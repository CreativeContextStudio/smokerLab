Here's a **tight, step‑by‑step guide** for **smoked and braised beef chuck roast** on your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S offset**, focused on tender, shreddable meat with deep beefy flavor and good bark. This assumes a **3–4 lb chuck roast** smoked at 250°F, then wrapped and braised low and slow. Always use **probe tenderness** (slides in like room‑temperature butter) to decide the final pull.

***

## 1. Choose and prep the chuck roast

- **Target:** 3–4 lb boneless beef chuck roast with visible intermuscular fat and marbling.
- **Trim:**
  - Remove any large ragged pieces or papery silver skin, but leave the intermuscular fat intact — it needs time to render and that's where the flavor lives.

**Do's**
- Pat dry with paper towels before seasoning.
- Apply a **salt‑and‑pepper rub** (coarse 16‑mesh black pepper first, then seasoned salt) 20–25 minutes before the cook and let it sweat until the surface looks wet and glossy. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't over‑trim; the intermuscular fat is what makes this cut taste like brisket once it breaks down.
- Don't skip the sweat time; you want the salt to activate and the surface to look shiny before it hits the smoker.

***

## 2. Set up the Royal Gourmet smoker

- **Target temp:** 250°F.
- **Fire:**
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox, building a **minion‑style fire** (2/3 unlit, 1/3 lit) with 1–2 small wood chunks (oak, hickory, or pecan). [best practice]
- **Vents:**
  - Stack **three‑quarters open**; firebox intake **1/4–1/3 open** to hold 250°F. [best practice]
- **Pan:**
  - Place a shallow water pan on the grate on the **stack side** to buffer temps. [best practice]

**Do's**
- Double‑check that **door gaskets and seams are sealed**. [best practice]
- Use **baffle / tuning plates** so heat travels smoothly from firebox to stack side. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't open the lid during the warm‑up phase.
- Don't crank the firebox vent wide open and the stack closed; that creates chaos.

## SM045 Setup (smoke phase)

Set your SM045 to **225–250°F** and preheat fully. Load **1–2 oz of oak, hickory, or pecan chunks** in the wood box at the start of the cook.

Place the seasoned chuck roast fat-side up on the middle rack. Insert the meat probe into the thickest part. Close the door.

No spritzing needed in the SM045. The sealed cabinet keeps surface moisture high on its own. Smoke the roast **3–4 hours** until you have a dark red to dark brown bark and the internal temp is around 165–175°F.

Pull the roast off the SM045 and transfer to a covered Dutch oven with 1–2 cups of beef stock or tallow for the braise. The oven braise stage is identical regardless of what smoker you used — it's the two-stage smoke-then-braise approach that makes this cut taste like brisket.

***

## 3. Place the chuck roast on the offset

- **Position:** Stack side of the chamber, not next to the firebox.
- **Orientation:**
  - Push the edges in and shape it into a nice, tight round — how you set it on is how it'll look when it's done.
  - Fat‑side up is preferred for a chuck roast to let fat baste down through the meat. [best practice]

**Do's**
- Insert a **meat‑probe thermometer** into the thickest part, avoiding big fat pockets.
- Insert a **grate‑level probe** near the meat to track smoker temp. [best practice]
- Close the lid and **do not open it for 1.5–2 hours** to let the bark start forming. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't put the chuck roast on the hottest side of the grill.
- Don't move it around during the first part of the cook.

***

## 4. Smoke naked to build bark (2–3 hours)

- **Goal:** Dark red to dark brown bark with good color; internal temp ~165–175°F.
- **Spritz:**
  - After 2 hours, open the lid **quickly** and spritz the surface with beef broth or a 50/50 broth‑water mix. Repeat every 30–45 minutes if the surface looks dry. [best practice]

**Do's**
- Focus on getting good color and bark — you're not looking for a specific internal temp during this phase, just a nice dark appearance. [best practice]
- Keep the smoke clean; thin blue smoke, not billowing white.

**Don'ts**
- Don't wrap before you have the color you want.
- Don't keep the lid open for extended checks.

***

## 5. Wrap and braise (3–4+ hours in the oven)

- **Goal:** Break down the intermuscular fat and collagen until the meat shreds effortlessly.
- **Wrap method:**
  - Once the bark is set and the roast has good color (~165–175°F), pull it off the smoker.
  - Place the chuck roast in a deep oven‑safe pan or Dutch oven.
  - Add **1–2 cups of beef stock or beef tallow** to the pan. [best practice]
  - Cover tightly with foil or a lid.
- **Oven temp:** 200°F for 3–4 hours, or as low as your oven will go (160–200°F) for up to 12–14 hours overnight. [best practice]

| Method | Oven temp | Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Quick braise | 200°F | 3–4 hours |
| Overnight braise | 160–200°F (as low as it goes) | 8–14 hours |

**Do's**
- Cover the pan **tightly** — you're braising, not roasting. [best practice]
- If braising overnight, check tenderness in the morning; it should shred with zero resistance.

**Don'ts**
- Don't braise uncovered; the moisture is critical for breaking down connective tissue.
- Don't worry about overcooking — the whole point is to shred it, and the braising liquid protects it.

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (3–4 lb chuck roast)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your roast's behavior.

| Time since fire‑up | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Fire up the CC1830S; build a minion‑fire, stabilize at 250°F. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | Load the chuck roast on the stack side, push edges in tight, close the lid. [best practice] |
| 2 hours | Quick spritz; check bark and color. If still light, keep smoking and spritz every 30–45 min. [best practice] |
| 2–3 hours | Continue smoking; look for dark red/brown bark and internal temp around 165–175°F. |
| ~3 hours | Pull off the smoker, place in a deep pan with beef stock or tallow, cover tightly. [best practice] |
| 3–6 hours (oven at 200°F) | Braise covered in the oven. Check tenderness after 3 hours — it should shred easily. |
| **OR** overnight (oven at 160–200°F) | Let it go 8–14 hours for hands‑off results. |

**Target finish:** Fork‑tender, shreds effortlessly, tastes like chopped brisket.

***

## 7. Resting and shredding

**Do's**
- Once tender, pull the pan out of the oven and let it rest on the countertop for **30 minutes to 1 hour** before shredding. [best practice]
- Shred with two forks or gloved hands; keep some chunky pieces for texture.
- Toss the shredded meat with some of the braising liquid (beef stock or tallow drippings) for moisture and flavor. [best practice]
- Serve over white rice with brown gravy, on sandwiches, or as a brisket‑style plate.

**Don'ts**
- Don't over‑shred into dust; keep some nice texture and chunks.
- Don't discard the braising liquid; it's liquid gold for gravy or dipping.
- Don't rush the rest — let the juices redistribute.

***

## 8. Do's / Don'ts for chuck roast on your offset

**Do's**
- Season with 16‑mesh black pepper first, then seasoned salt, and let it sweat 20–25 minutes. [best practice]
- Keep the smoker sealed, use baffle/tuning plates, and manage temps via the firebox intake. [best practice]
- Smoke for **color and bark**, then braise for **tenderness** — the two‑stage approach is what makes this taste like brisket.
- Use a **thermometer** during the smoke phase and **probe tenderness** for the braise. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't try to smoke it all the way to tender on the pit; this cut needs a braise to fully break down.
- Don't open the lid constantly during the smoke phase.
- Don't skip the braising liquid; dry heat alone won't give you the brisket‑like result.
