Here’s a **tight, step‑by‑step guide** for smoked swordfish on your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S** at **225–250°F**, with **do’s / don’ts** and time targets for **1–2 inch thick swordfish steaks (about 1–2 lbs total)**. This method focuses on **moist, flaky swordfish with a light smoke flavor** and optional crispy skin.

***

## 1. Prep the swordfish (30–60 minutes before smoking)

- **Choose the swordfish:**  
  - Fresh or thawed swordfish steaks, about **1–2 inches thick** and 6–8 ounces each.  
- **Trim and pat dry:**  
  - Remove any dark connective tissue and skin (if you don’t want it).  
  - Rinse under cold water and pat extremely dry with paper towels.  

**Do’s**  
- Leave any skin on if you want a crispy, grilled‑style exterior.  
- Let the swordfish sit uncovered in the fridge for **10–20 minutes** to dry the surface slightly (optional).  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t leave the swordfish at room temp for long; keep it cold until you’re ready to smoke.  
- Don’t skip the drying step; wet fish = soggy steaks.  

***

## 2. Season the swordfish (10–15 minutes before smoking)

- **Simple seasoning:**  
  - Salt, pepper, garlic powder, and a pinch of paprika.  
  - Optional: A light glaze of **olive oil** or **brown sugar** for a candy‑crust effect.  

**Do’s**  
- Coat the swordfish steaks generously on both sides, focusing on the flesh.  
- Let it sit at room temp for **10–15 minutes** to let the seasoning penetrate.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t oversalt; the ocean already adds salt.  
- Don’t apply a thick glaze; keep it light for a delicate smoke flavor.  

***

## 3. Set up the Royal Gourmet smoker

- **Target temp:** 225–250°F for swordfish (this is **hotter** than brisket, which is fine for a small fish).  
- **Fire:**  
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox, building a **hot charcoal bed** (no minion‑style; this is a fast, high‑heat cook).  
  - Add 1–2 small mild wood chunks (apple, maple, or oak) for a light smoke flavor. [best practice]  
- **Vents:**  
  - Stack **fully open**; firebox intake **1/2–3/4 open** to reach 225–250°F. [best practice]  
- **Pan:**  
  - Place a shallow water pan under the grate opposite the heat source to catch drips and keep the smoker clean. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Let the smoker come to temp **before** you load the swordfish.  
- Use a **grate‑level thermometer** near the swordfish to monitor the surface temp.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t start the swordfish too early; the smoker should be hot and stable before you load it.  
- Don’t overcrowd the grate; swordfish steaks need space for even air circulation.  

***

## 4. Place the swordfish on the offset

- **Position:** Stack side of the chamber, not directly over the hottest coals.  
- **Orientation:**  
  - Place the swordfish steaks **skin‑side down** on the grate for a crispy exterior.  
  - If you’re worried about the skin sticking, place a **thin sheet of parchment paper** under the steaks.  

**Do’s**  
- Use **tongs or heat‑resistant gloves** to place the swordfish gently on the grate.  
- Double‑check that the skin is not stuck to the grate before you close the lid.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t put the swordfish directly over the hottest coals; it can burn the skin.  
- Don’t move them around once they’re on the grate.  

***

## 5. Smoke time and temp targets (225–250°F)

**Goal:** 140–145°F internal at the thickest part, with **flaky, moist meat** and optional **crispy skin**. [best practice]  

| Temp range | Time (approx) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 225–250°F | 15–25 minutes | Great for 1–2 swordfish steaks, with a nice balance of smoke and tenderness. |

**Do’s**  
- Use a **meat‑probe thermometer** inserted into the thickest part of the steak.  
- Check the temp every 5–10 minutes after the first 10 minutes.  
- If the skin is getting too dark, move the steaks to a cooler part of the grate or slightly lower the temp.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check the swordfish.  
- Don’t let the internal temp exceed 145°F; it can dry out.  

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (1–2 swordfish steaks at 225–250°F)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your swordfish’s behavior.

| Time since loading the swordfish | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–10 min | Load the swordfish steaks skin‑side down on the offset, close the lid. [best practice] |
| 10–15 min | No opening; let the swordfish come to temp and the smoke start to build. |
| 15–20 min | Check the internal temp; it should be 110–120°F. If it’s on the lower side, keep going. |
| 20–22 min | Check again; if it’s 130–140°F, start thinking about finishing. |
| 22–25 min | Check again; if it’s 140–145°F, remove the swordfish. [best practice] |

**Target finish:** 140–145°F internal, **flaky and moist** with optional **crispy skin**.  

***

## 7. Finish with a crispy sear (optional but highly recommended)

- **Option A (internal crisp):**  
  - Increase the smoker temp to 300–350°F for the last 2–5 minutes to crisp the skin.  
  - Keep the lid closed and watch for the skin to turn golden and crackle.  

- **Option B (external sear):**  
  - Remove the swordfish at 140–145°F and let it rest for 5–10 minutes.  
  - Then, pan‑sear the skin side in a hot skillet with a little oil for 1–2 minutes per side for a super‑crispy crust.  

**Do’s**  
- Use a **thermometer** to confirm 140–145°F before you finish.  
- Let the swordfish rest for 5–10 minutes before serving to let the juices redistribute.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t let the internal temp exceed 145°F; it can dry out.  
- Don’t skip the rest; it’s critical for juicy meat.  

***

## 8. Do’s / Don’ts for smoked swordfish on your offset

**Do’s**  
- Use a **sharp fillet knife** for trimming.  
- Keep the skin on and dry before seasoning.  
- Use a **thermometer** and **probe tenderness** as your final decision‑maker. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t remove the skin; it’s key for moisture and support.  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check the swordfish.  
- Don’t skip the rest after cooking; it’s critical for juicy meat.
