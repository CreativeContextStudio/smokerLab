Here's a **tight, step‑by‑step guide** for **smoked beef cheeks (barbacoa‑style)** on your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S offset**, focused on rich, shreddable meat with intense beefy flavor from an overnight confit in beef tallow. This assumes **3–4 lb of beef cheeks** (2–4 cheeks depending on size) smoked at 250°F for color, then confited overnight. Always use **probe tenderness** to decide when they're done.

***

## 1. Choose and prep the beef cheeks

- **Target:** 3–4 lb of beef cheeks from the butcher counter or meat department.
- **Trim:**
  - Remove large chunks of excess external fat — the big, thick pads that won't render down. **Save this fat** for sausage making or rendering into tallow later.
  - Leave the internal tendons, collagen, and smaller fat deposits alone; they will break down during the confit and create richness.

**Do's**
- Pat dry with paper towels before seasoning.
- Apply a **heavy coat of 16‑mesh black pepper first, then seasoned salt** — go a little heavier on the salt than you normally would, because the overnight tallow bath will mellow out the salt profile. [best practice]
- Let the seasoned cheeks sit out for **15–20 minutes** until the surface looks wet and glossy. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't try to make these look pretty — they came from the cheek of a cow. Focus on getting the big inedible fat off and seasoning well.
- Don't throw away the trimmed fat; freeze it for sausage or tallow.

***

## 2. Set up the Royal Gourmet smoker

- **Target temp:** 250°F.
- **Fire:**
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox, building a **minion‑style fire** (2/3 unlit, 1/3 lit) with 1–2 small wood chunks (oak, hickory, or mesquite). [best practice]
- **Vents:**
  - Stack **three‑quarters open**; firebox intake **1/4–1/3 open** to hold 250°F. [best practice]
- **Pan:**
  - Place a shallow water pan on the grate on the **stack side** to buffer temps. [best practice]

**Do's**
- Double‑check that **door gaskets and seams are sealed**. [best practice]
- Use **baffle / tuning plates** so heat travels smoothly from firebox to stack side. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't open the lid during the warm‑up phase.
- Don't crank the firebox vent wide open and the stack closed; that creates chaos.

***

## 3. Place the beef cheeks on the offset

- **Position:** Closer to the firebox side or the heat exchange area — beef cheeks can handle more heat and you really can't stress this cut out. [best practice]
- **Orientation:**
  - Lay them flat, fat‑trimmed side up if there's one smoother side.

**Do's**
- Insert a **grate‑level probe** near the cheeks to track smoker temp. [best practice]
- Close the lid and **do not open it for 1.5–2 hours** to let the bark form. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't overthink placement — beef cheeks are forgiving and can take a good bit of direct heat.
- Don't move them around during the first part of the cook.

***

## 4. Smoke naked to build bark and color (2–3 hours)

- **Goal:** Dark, smoky bark with good color; internal temp ~165–175°F.
- **What to watch for:**
  - You're smoking for **color only** on this protein — don't worry about hitting a specific internal temp. You just want a nice dark red to dark brown tone before pulling. [best practice]
- **Spritz:**
  - After 2 hours, open the lid **quickly** and spritz with beef broth if the surface looks dry. Repeat every 30–45 minutes. [best practice]

**Do's**
- Focus entirely on bark color — once you have a dark, smoky exterior, they're ready to come off the pit. [best practice]
- Keep the smoke clean; thin blue smoke, not billowing white.

**Don'ts**
- Don't wrap these on the smoker; they're going straight into the confit after smoking.
- Don't keep the lid open for long checks.

***

## 5. Confit overnight in beef tallow (8–14 hours)

- **Goal:** Break down all the tendons, collagen, and connective tissue until the cheeks shred like butter.
- **Confit method:**
  - Once the cheeks have good bark and color, pull them off the smoker.
  - Place them in a deep oven‑safe pan or Dutch oven.
  - Pour **melted beef tallow** over the cheeks until they are mostly submerged. If you don't have tallow, use **beef stock** — you'll get the same tender result with slightly less richness. [best practice]
  - Cover the pan **tightly** with foil or a lid.
- **Oven temp:** 200°F for 3–4 hours, or as low as your oven will go (140–200°F) for an overnight confit of 8–14 hours. [best practice]

| Method | Oven temp | Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Quick confit | 200°F | 3–4 hours |
| Overnight confit (recommended) | 140–200°F (as low as it goes) | 8–14 hours |

**Do's**
- Cover the pan **tightly** — the seal matters. [best practice]
- If going overnight, set the oven before bed and check in the morning; they should shred with absolutely no resistance.
- If you finish early and are still hours from serving, pull the pan out and let it sit on the countertop — it'll stay very hot for a couple of hours. [best practice]

**Don'ts**
- Don't confit uncovered; the tallow or stock bath is what makes this cut transform.
- Don't worry about overcooking — beef cheeks only get better the longer they braise.

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (3–4 lb beef cheeks)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your cheeks' behavior.

| Time since fire‑up | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Fire up the CC1830S; build a minion‑fire, stabilize at 250°F. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | Load the beef cheeks on the smoker, closer to the heat exchange area, close the lid. [best practice] |
| 2 hours | Quick spritz; check bark and color. If still light, keep smoking and spritz every 30–45 min. [best practice] |
| 2–3 hours | Continue smoking; look for dark, smoky bark. Don't worry about internal temp — focus on color. |
| ~3 hours | Pull off the smoker, place in a deep pan, cover with melted beef tallow or beef stock. Cover tightly. [best practice] |
| Evening | Place the covered pan in the oven at 200°F (or as low as it goes) before bed. |
| Next morning (8–14 hours later) | Check tenderness — the cheeks should shred with absolutely no resistance. Pull from oven. |

**Target finish:** Shreds effortlessly like chuck roast on steroids — super rich with intense beefy flavor.

***

## 7. Resting and shredding

**Do's**
- Pull the pan from the oven and let it rest for **20–30 minutes** before shredding. [best practice]
- Shred with two forks or gloved hands; beef cheeks will fall apart almost on their own.
- Toss the shredded barbacoa with some of the confit liquid for extra moisture and richness. [best practice]
- Serve in barbacoa rice bowls with cilantro lime rice and green verde crema, in tacos, burritos, or on its own.

**Don'ts**
- Don't over‑shred; keep some nice chunky pieces for texture.
- Don't discard the tallow or braising liquid; strain and save it for future cooks.
- Don't rush the overnight confit — the long, slow braise is what makes barbacoa barbacoa.

***

## 8. Do's / Don'ts for beef cheeks on your offset

**Do's**
- Season with 16‑mesh black pepper first, then go **heavier than normal on the seasoned salt** — the tallow bath will mellow it out. [best practice]
- Smoke for **color and bark only**, then confit for **tenderness** — the two‑stage approach is what makes this incredible.
- Use **beef tallow** for the confit if you have it; beef stock works as a substitute. [best practice]
- Save trimmed fat for sausage or rendering into tallow.

**Don'ts**
- Don't try to smoke beef cheeks all the way to tender on the pit; the confit stage is essential.
- Don't open the lid constantly during the smoke phase.
- Don't skip the overnight confit — a 3–4 hour quick braise works, but overnight is where the magic happens.
