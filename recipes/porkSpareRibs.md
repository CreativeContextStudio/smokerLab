Here’s a **tight, step‑by‑step guide** for **smoked pork spare ribs** on your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S** at **225–250°F**, with **do’s / don’ts** and time targets for a full rack of **spareribs (3–4 lbs untrimmed)**. You can easily scale this up or down.

***

## 1. Prep the ribs (1–2 hours before smoking)

- **Choose the ribs:**  
  - Full rack of **pork spare ribs**, with the **membrane intact**.  
- **Remove the membrane (optional but recommended):**  
  - Slide a knife under the membrane on the back of the rack, then grab it with a paper towel and peel it off. This helps bark and flavor penetrate. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Trim excess fat and the thin flap of brisket‑style meat along the top.  
- Pat the ribs dry with paper towels.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t leave the membrane on if you want better bark and flavor.  
- Don’t over‑trim and slice off all fat; keep the fat cap for moisture.  

***

## 2. Season the ribs (1–2 hours before smoking)

- **Choose a rub:**  
  - Classic option: 2:1 salt/pepper, garlic powder, onion powder, paprika, brown sugar, and a touch of cayenne.  
  - Optional: Add a light layer of brown sugar or honey for a candy‑crust effect.  

**Do’s**  
- Coat the ribs generously on both sides, focusing on the meaty side.  
- Let them sit uncovered in the fridge for **1–2 hours** to let the rub penetrate.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t oversalt; you can always add more later.  
- Don’t apply a thick glaze yet; keep it dry for the smoke.  

***

## 3. Set up the Royal Gourmet smoker

- **Target temp:** 225–250°F for pork spare ribs.
- **Fire:**
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox, building a **minion‑style fire** (2/3 unlit, 1/3 lit) with 1–2 small wood chunks (apple, hickory, or maple). [best practice]
- **Vents:**
  - Stack **mostly open**; firebox intake **1/4–1/3 open** to hold 225–250°F. [best practice]
- **Pan:**
  - Place a shallow water pan on the grate on the **stack side** to buffer temps. [best practice]

**Do’s**
- Let the smoker come to temp **before** you load the ribs.
- Use a **grate‑level thermometer** near the ribs to monitor the surface temp.

**Don’ts**
- Don’t start the ribs too early; the smoker should be hot and stable before you load them.
- Don’t overcrowd the grate; ribs need space for even air circulation.

## SM045 Setup

Set your SM045 to **225–240°F** and preheat fully. Load **1–2 oz of apple or hickory chunks** in the wood box. That is your one and only wood charge.

Place the rack meat-side up on the middle rack. Spare ribs are larger than baby backs — if the rack doesn’t fit flat, cut it in half or curve the ends up slightly. Close the door.

No spritzing. The SM045 is humid enough that the ribs stay moist without any intervention. Budget **5–7 hours** for a full spare rib rack — spare ribs take longer than baby backs because of their size and thicker bones.

Optional wrap: around hours 3–4, you can wrap in foil to push through the stall and speed up cooking. Shorten the wrap phase by 20–30 minutes versus an offset because of the SM045’s higher ambient humidity.

Judge doneness by the **bend test** and **bone pullback**. The rack is done when it droops significantly when lifted at one end with tongs, and at least 1/4 inch of clean bone is showing at the tips.

## 4. Place the ribs on the offset

- **Position:** Stack side of the chamber, not directly over the hottest coals.  
- **Orientation:**  
  - Place the ribs **meat‑side up** so the bark forms on the top.  
  - Arrange them in a single layer, not stacked.  

**Do’s**  
- Use **tongs or a heat‑resistant glove** to place the ribs gently on the grate.  
- Double‑check that the skin is not stuck to the grate before you close the lid.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t put the ribs directly over the hottest coals; it can burn the bark.  
- Don’t move them around once they’re on the grate.  

***

## 5. Smoke time and temp targets (225–250°F)

**Goal:** 195–205°F internal, with **meat that pulls back from the bones** and a **crisp bark**. [best practice]  

| Temp range | Time (approx) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 225–250°F | 4–6 hours | Great for a 3–4 lb rack of ribs, with a nice balance of smoke and tenderness. |

**Do’s**  
- Use a **meat‑probe thermometer** inserted into the thickest part of the rack.  
- Check the temp every 30–45 minutes after the first 2 hours.  
- If the ribs are still tough, let them cook longer; don’t rush the bark.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check the ribs.  
- Don’t let the internal temp exceed 210°F; it can dry out.  

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (3–4 lb rack of ribs at 225–250°F)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your ribs’ behavior.

| Time since loading the ribs | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Load the ribs meat‑side up on the offset, close the lid. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | No opening; let the ribs come to temp and the smoke start to build. |
| 1–2 hours | Spritz the ribs with a 50/50 mix of water and apple juice every 30–45 minutes. |
| 2–3 hours | Check the bark; if it’s starting to set, you can start thinking about wrapping. |
| 3–4 hours | Wrap the ribs in foil or butcher paper with a splash of liquid (apple juice, broth, or water). |
| 4–6 hours | Let them cook wrapped; check the temp every 30–45 minutes. [best practice] |

**Target finish:** 195–205°F internal, with **meat that pulls back from the bones** and a **crisp bark**.  

***

## 7. Finish and rest the ribs

- **Option A (unwrapped ribs):**  
  - Remove the ribs at 195–205°F and let them rest for 15–30 minutes before slicing.  
- **Option B (wrapped ribs):**  
  - Unwrap the ribs at 195–205°F, place them back on the smoker for 15–30 minutes to crisp the bark, then let them rest.  

**Do’s**  
- Let the ribs rest for at least 15 minutes before slicing.  
- Slice the ribs into 2–3 pieces per rack for easy serving.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t slice them immediately off the smoker; they’ll be tough.  
- Don’t skip the rest; it’s critical for juicy meat.  

***

## 8. Do’s / Don’ts for smoked pork spare ribs on your offset

**Do’s**  
- Use a **sharp boning or kitchen shears** for trimming.  
- Keep the skin intact and dry before seasoning.  
- Use a **thermometer** and **probe tenderness** as your final decision‑maker. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t leave the membrane on if you want better bark and flavor.  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check the ribs.  
- Don’t skip the rest after cooking; it’s critical for juicy meat.
