**Morcilla** is Uruguay\u2019s most traditional asado starter \u2014 a blood sausage seasoned with onion, spices, and sometimes rice, cooked gently on the cool edge of the parrilla until the skin tightens and the interior turns warm and creamy. It arrives at the table with bread before the main cuts, and it is the signal that the asado has truly begun. For 6\u20138 guests, plan 3\u20134 links alongside the chorizo.

***

## Fuego / Fire Setup

Morcilla is the most delicate item on the parrilla. It cannot take direct high heat \u2014 the casing splits and the filling dries out.

1. The brasero should be in its early phase \u2014 a good fire with some developing coals, but not at peak ember production. Morcilla goes on first, before the main beef cuts.
2. **Set the grate at HIGH position** \u2014 15\u201320 cm (6\u20138 inches) above the embers, or position the morcilla at the **cooler edge of the grate**, away from the direct heat zone.
3. You want gentle, indirect warmth \u2014 the morcilla is essentially warming through and its skin tightening, not charring.

**Timing in the asado:** Morcilla and chorizo go on together as the very first items, while the fire is still settling and guests are arriving. They cook slowly at the edge of the grate while the asador focuses on getting the main cuts seasoned and ready.

**Do\u2019s**
- Use the perimeter of the grate \u2014 the coolest zone, away from the hottest coals.
- Rotate gently every 4\u20135 minutes.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t put morcilla directly over the hottest coal zone \u2014 it will burst and lose everything.
- Don\u2019t use tongs aggressively \u2014 morcilla casing is fragile. Use a flat spatula or roll gently.

***

## Preparaci\u00F3n / Meat Prep

1. Use **morcilla criolla** \u2014 the Uruguayan-style blood sausage made with pork blood, onion, fat, and spices. It is softer and more delicate than European-style blood sausage.
2. No seasoning needed \u2014 the morcilla is fully seasoned inside.
3. Remove from refrigeration **15\u201320 minutes** before cooking.
4. Handle gently \u2014 the casing is fragile when cold.

**Do\u2019s**
- If the morcilla is in a natural casing, handle with care even at room temperature.
- Keep them in a single layer on the grate \u2014 don\u2019t stack.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t prick or score the casing. The filling must stay inside.
- Don\u2019t cook from frozen.

***

## Secuencia de Parrilla / Grill Sequence

### Phase 1: Gentle warming (12\u201318 min)

1. Place morcilla on the **cool edge of the high grate**, away from the direct coal zone.
2. The goal is to warm the filling through entirely while the skin tightens and takes on a slight colour.
3. Rotate every **4\u20135 minutes** using a flat spatula \u2014 roll gently, don\u2019t grip.
4. The morcilla is ready when:
   - The skin is uniformly tightened, slightly firm, and has taken on a deeper colour (from dark red to dark brown)
   - It feels warm to a gentle press \u2014 firm throughout, not soft and cold in the center
   - The ends begin to look slightly caramelised

**Sensory cues:**
- Skin tightening and pulling: on track
- Colour deepening to dark brown: nearly ready
- Firm but yielding under gentle pressure with a spatula: done
- Skin splitting slightly at one end: this is acceptable \u2014 it means it\u2019s fully heated through

**If the skin does burst open:** This is common with morcilla criolla. Don\u2019t panic \u2014 pull it off gently, the filling is still good, and the split portion can be scooped onto bread with a spoon. It is not a failure; it\u2019s a common occurrence even at the finest parrillas.

### Phase 2: Serve immediately

- Morcilla does not benefit from resting. Serve directly from the grate onto a board.
- Provide bread alongside \u2014 guests spread or scoop the morcilla onto bread, or eat the link whole.

***

## Servicio y Acompa\u00F1amientos / Serving and Sides

- **Serve with crusty bread** \u2014 marraqueta or baguette slices.
- A small dish of **chimichurri** alongside is welcomed by some, though purists prefer morcilla unadulterated.
- **Sequence:** Serves alongside chorizo as the asado\u2019s opening act, well before the main beef cuts arrive.
- **Drink:** Cold beer is the classic companion to morcilla in Uruguay. Or a small glass of Tannat.

**Cultural note:** Morcilla is the cut that divides first-timers from regulars at the asado. The newcomer hesitates; the regular reaches for it first, spreads it on warm bread, and settles into a chair to wait for the rest. In Montevideo, a parrilla that runs out of morcilla before the main cuts arrive is a parrilla that didn\u2019t plan well.

***

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Position morcilla at the coolest edge of the grate, away from intense heat.
- Rotate every 4\u20135 minutes with a flat spatula \u2014 gently.
- Serve as soon as it\u2019s warmed through and the skin has tightened.
- Provide plenty of crusty bread.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t cook over direct high heat \u2014 the casing will burst and the filling will dry out.
- Don\u2019t prick the casing.
- Don\u2019t grip with tongs \u2014 the casing is fragile. Use a spatula.
- Don\u2019t overcook \u2014 10\u201315 minutes of gentle heat is enough. Extended cooking dries it out.
