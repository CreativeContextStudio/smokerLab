Perfect—**pulled pork** is an ideal first goal on your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S offset** with a 3–4 lb pork butt. Here’s a **step‑by‑step, timing‑based script** focused on juicy, bark‑forward pulled pork, tuned for your setup.  

All times are approximate; always use the **meat‑probe tenderness rule** (slider like room‑temperature butter) to decide the final cut‑off. [meatchurch](https://www.meatchurch.com/blogs/recipes/brisket)

***

## 1. Prep (Night before or 30–60 min before you light)

- **Pork butt:** 3–4 lb Boston butt, fat cap 1/4–3/8", excess ragged edges trimmed but not shaved to bone.  
- **Rub:** 1:1 salt/pepper, paprika, garlic powder, onion powder, pinch of brown sugar.  
- **Do’s:**  
  - Trim and pat dry.  
  - Apply rub generously; let sit 30–60 min before you light the fire.  
- **Don’ts:**  
  - Don’t over‑trim or slice off all fat.  
  - Don’t use too much sugar if you plan long, low cook (225–250°F).  

***

## 2. Fire up and stabilize (225–250°F)

- Start your King Charcoal / Royal Gourmet coal pan in the firebox.
- **Fire setup:**
  - 2/3 unlit charcoal in the basket, 1/3 lit from chimney dumped on one side (minion‑style).
  - Add 1–2 wood chunks (apple, hickory, pecan) once the smoker reads ~200°F. [best practice]
- **Vents:**
  - Stack fully open.
  - Firebox intake about 1/2 open at first, then dial to 1/4–1/3 once you hit 225–250°F. [best practice]

**Do’s:**
- Double‑check that **door gaskets** and **baffle/tuning plates** seal the chamber.
- Place a **water pan** on the grate on the “stack side” to buffer temps. [best practice]

**Don’ts:**
- Don’t open the lid during this 20–30 min warm‑up.
- Don’t use whole logs or too much wood in the firebox.

## SM045 Setup

Set your SM045 to **235–250°F** and preheat with the door closed for 30–60 minutes. Load **2 oz of hickory or apple chunks** in the wood box at the start — this is your complete wood charge for the entire cook.

Place the butt fat-side up on the middle rack. Insert the meat probe into the thickest part, avoiding large fat pockets. Close the door.

**No spritzing needed.** The SM045’s sealed cabinet naturally keeps surface moisture high. The wood box smolders without an open flame, producing clean smoke throughout the cook.

The stall will likely be longer in the SM045 than on an offset because the humid environment reduces the evaporative effect that normally breaks the stall. Budget **6–10 hours** for a 3–4 lb butt. When the internal temp stalls around 165–175°F, wrap tightly in butcher paper — this is still the best tool for pushing through it.

Begin checking for probe tenderness at 5 hours. Pull at **198–205°F probe-tender**. Rest wrapped for 1–2 hours before pulling.

## 3. Place the butt and start the cook (Set & forget 1–2 hours)

- Put the butt **fat‑side up, on the stack side** of the offset, not next to the firebox.  
- **Probe placement:**  
  - Meat probe into the thickest part, avoiding big fat pockets.  
  - Grate probe near the butt but not directly over the firebox opening. [best practice]  

**Do’s:**  
- Close the lid and **do not open it for 1.5–2 hours** (no peeking, no photos). [best practice]  
- Let it sit at 225–250°F while the bark starts to form.  

**Don’ts:**  
- Don’t move the butt around or rotate it constantly.  
- Don’t adjust vents every 10 minutes; let changes settle 15 min before tweaking again. [best practice]  

***

## 4. Spritz and check bark (Hours 2–3)

- After 2 hours, open the lid **quickly** and:  
  - Spritz the surface with **apple juice, cider, or a 50/50 beef broth‑water mix**.  
  - Repeat every 30–45 minutes depending on how dry it looks.  
- Watch for the 3 key signs it’s time to **wrap or keep naked**:

| Signal | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Dark, tacky bark, rub locked on | You can keep it naked for more bark‑forward pulled pork. [best practice] |
| Bark looks great but meat is getting tough | Wrap in **butcher paper** (or foil) to protect bark and speed cook. [best practice] |
| Barely set bark, still light‑colored | Keep it naked a bit longer; don’t wrap too early. [best practice] |

For a **small butt going to pull**, wrapping around **170–175°F internal** (often at ~2.5–3 hours) is a solid sweet spot. [smokejustis](https://www.smokejustis.com/smokes-blog/avoid-these-4-mistakes-when-smoking-brisket)

**Do’s:**  
- Use a **big sheet of butcher paper** (or 2 foil sheets) for a tight wrap with the drag‑and‑fold method.  
- Place a **tiny splash of liquid** (apple juice, broth, or a little water) on the inside of the paper.  

**Don’ts:**  
- Don’t wrap while the bark is still pink‑looking or powdery.  
- Don’t over‑soak the paper with liquid; you’re not boiling the butt.  

***

## 5. Cook times and temps (3–4 lb pulled pork at 225–250°F)

Use these as ballpark guides, but **always check tenderness**:

| Situation | Time to 195°F / tenderness |
| --- | --- |
| Naked only | 4–5 hours |  
| Naked → wrapped at ~2.5–3 hours | 2.5–3 hours wrapped after |  

Once wrapped:

- Butt usually goes from **175°F to 200°F in 2–3 hours** at 225–250°F. [urbancowgirllife](https://urbancowgirllife.com/how-long-to-cook-a-brisket-at-250/)

**Do’s:**  
- Probe at 180–185°F, then every 30–45 minutes until **195–205°F and probe‑tender**.  
- Leave the **exhaust stack open** and only tweak the **firebox intake** if the pit is trending up or down. [best practice]  

**Don’ts:**  
- Don’t pull it at 190°F if it’s still firm.  
- Don’t overshoot to 210–215°F; it can get mushy.  

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute pulled‑pork script (3–4 lb)

Use this as a mental script; adjust as your butt talks to you.

| Time since fire‑up | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Fire up, light chimney, build minion‑fire, let pit stabilize at 225–250°F. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | Put the butt on the smoker and close the lid. [best practice] |
| 2 hours | Quick spritz; check bark; if it’s still light, spritz every 30–45 min. [best practice] |
| 2.5–3 hours | Re‑check bark and temp.  
  - If bark is dark and rub has set (~170–175°F), **wrap in butcher paper**.  
  - If bark is still light, keep it naked and wrap later around 175–180°F. [best practice] |
| 3–4.5 hours (if wrapped early) | Let it cook.  
  - Keep 225–250°F, adjust vents minimally.  
  - Probe at 180–185°F, then 190°F, then 195°F. [best practice] |
| 4.5–5.5 hours (if naked) | Probe every 30–45 min once internal gets above 185°F. [best practice] |

**Target finish:** 195–205°F internal, **probe‑tender**.  

***

## 7. Resting and pulling

**Do’s:**  
- Pull when **probe‑tender** (195–205°F), keep it fully wrapped, and rest **at least 1.5–2 hours** on the counter. [best practice]  
- After resting:  
  - Open the wrap carefully, pour the juices into a bowl.  
  - Pull the meat into chunks with forks or wear gloves and pull/shred.  
  - Toss the pulled pork with a little of the collected juices for moisture and flavor. [best practice]  

**Don’ts:**  
- Don’t slice or pull it right off the smoker.  
- Don’t over‑shred into dust; keep some nice texture.  
- Don’t discard all the juices; they hold great flavor.  

***

## Quick “Do’s / Don’ts” for pulled pork on your offset

**Do’s**  
- Seal the smoker, use a baffle, keep the stack mostly open, and manage temps via the firebox intake. [best practice]  
- Use a **charcoal + small wood chunks** combo, not a big log fire. [best practice]  
- Wrap around 170–175°F unless you specifically want extreme bark; treat it like a barbecue‑style pulled pork, not a Texas brisket bark monster. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check on the meat.  
- Don’t chase the clock instead of the probe tenderness.  
- Don’t skip the rest; that’s when juices redistribute and texture improves.
