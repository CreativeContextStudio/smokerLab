**Morrones asados** \u2014 charred and peeled bell peppers \u2014 are one of the most versatile sides in the Uruguayan asado repertoire. Whole peppers go directly on a hot grate over active embers, turning until their skins are blackened entirely, then steam in a covered bag until the skins slip off to reveal silky, sweet flesh underneath. Dressed with olive oil, sliced garlic, and a pinch of salt, they are superb at room temperature as a side dish, or used to build the filling for a pamplona. For 6\u20138 guests, plan 4\u20136 red peppers and 2\u20133 green.

***

## Fuego / Fire Setup

Morrones asados need **high, direct ember heat** to char the skins quickly before the flesh overcooks.

1. The grate should be at a **low-mid position** \u2014 approximately 8\u201312 cm (3\u20135 inches) above the ember bed.
2. The ember bed should be hot and active \u2014 this is a good job for the part of the asado where the main cuts are already on and the brasero has its peak coal production.
3. Alternatively, morrones can be placed directly on the brasero coal bed surface (not buried, just resting on top) \u2014 the direct contact with coals chars them even faster.

**Timing in the asado:** Morrones can go on the grate while the slow cuts are cooking and the asador needs to use all available heat. They take 20\u201325 minutes and can sit dressed at room temperature for an hour before serving.

**Do\u2019s**
- Char the skins completely \u2014 black all over is the goal. There is no such thing as too charred for morrones asados.
- Rotate every 3\u20134 minutes to char all sides.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t stop before the skins are fully black \u2014 partially charred peppers are harder to peel and less sweet.
- Don\u2019t use a cool or fading ember bed for this step \u2014 high, sustained heat produces the best char.

***

## Preparaci\u00F3n / Prep

1. Leave the peppers whole and intact \u2014 do not cut, score, or seed them before grilling.
2. No oil, no seasoning before grilling \u2014 the char is the entire process.

**Do\u2019s**
- Use large, thick-walled peppers \u2014 they char well and have plenty of flesh once peeled.
- Have a paper bag or a covered bowl ready for the steaming step after grilling.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t oil the peppers \u2014 it causes flare-ups and uneven charring.
- Don\u2019t cut them before grilling \u2014 the whole pepper steams in its own skin.

***

## Secuencia de Parrilla / Grill Sequence

### Phase 1: Charring on the grate (20\u201325 min)

1. Place whole peppers directly on the low-mid grate over the hot ember bed.
2. Rotate every **3\u20134 minutes** using long tongs.
3. Continue until **all sides are completely blackened** \u2014 there should be no green or red visible on the exterior.
4. Total charring time: 20\u201325 minutes for large peppers.

**Sensory cues:**
- Black, papery, blistered skin on all surfaces: done
- Peppers soften and begin to collapse slightly when pressed with tongs: done
- Steam escaping from the stem end: the flesh inside is fully cooked

### Phase 2: Steaming in the bag (10\u201315 min)

1. Remove the charred peppers from the grate and place them immediately into a **paper bag or covered bowl**.
2. Close the bag or cover the bowl tightly.
3. Let the peppers steam in their own heat for **10\u201315 minutes**. The steam loosens the charred skin from the flesh below.

### Phase 3: Peeling

1. Remove the peppers from the bag.
2. Hold each pepper over a bowl and slip off the charred skin with your fingers \u2014 it should slide off easily in large pieces.
3. Do not rinse under water \u2014 you will wash off some of the smoky flavour.
4. Tear or cut the peeled peppers open, remove the seeds and stem, and tear into wide strips.

**Do\u2019s**
- Work over a bowl to catch the sweet juices that run from the peppers during peeling.
- Save those juices \u2014 they are liquid gold for dressing.

**Don\u2019ts**
- Don\u2019t rinse the peppers under water.
- Don\u2019t rush the steaming step \u2014 insufficiently steamed peppers are harder to peel.

***

## Aliño / Dressing

In a wide shallow dish, arrange the torn pepper strips. Dress with:
- 3\u20134 tbsp good olive oil
- 2\u20133 thin slices of garlic
- Salt to taste
- Optional: 1 tbsp red wine vinegar and a pinch of dried oregano

Pour the collected pepper juices over the top. Let rest at room temperature for at least 15 minutes before serving.

***

## Servicio y Acompa\u00F1amientos / Serving

- Serve at room temperature on a platter alongside the main cuts.
- Excellent with crusty bread to soak up the olive oil and pepper juices.
- Used as a filling component for pamplona.
- Pairs particularly well with asado de tira and vac\u00EDo.

**Cultural note:** Morrones asados are the quiet genius of the parrilla. They take 25 minutes, they sit happily at room temperature, they improve as they rest in the olive oil, and they make any plate of grilled meat look intentional and generous. An asador who puts out morrones asados alongside the main cuts is an asador who has thought about the whole table, not just the grate.

***

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Char completely \u2014 all surfaces black.
- Steam in a closed bag for 10\u201315 minutes immediately after grilling.
- Do not rinse after peeling \u2014 keep the smoky flavour.
- Dress with good olive oil, sliced garlic, and the collected juices.
- Rest before serving \u2014 they improve with 15\u201330 minutes in the dressing.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t stop charring early \u2014 half-charred skins are much harder to peel.
- Don\u2019t oil before grilling.
- Don\u2019t rinse under water after peeling.
- Don\u2019t rush the steam step.
