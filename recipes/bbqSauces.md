**Regional BBQ sauces** are the fingerprint of American barbecue. Every region has its own answer to the question of what belongs on smoked meat, and the differences are not trivial. Kansas City builds thick, sweet, molasses-dark glazes. Eastern Carolina strips everything back to vinegar and pepper. South Carolina swears by mustard. Alabama puts mayonnaise on chicken and dares you to argue. Texas barely wants sauce at all.

These seven recipes each make about 2 cups. They all store well in the fridge for 1 to 2 weeks. Scale freely, adjust heat and sweetness to taste, and use them as finishing glazes, table sauces, or dipping sauces alongside whatever you pull off the fire.

***

## Kansas City Style

Kansas City sauce is the one most people picture when they hear "BBQ sauce." It is thick, sweet, tomato-and-molasses heavy, and designed to glaze ribs and chicken in the last 10 to 20 minutes of cooking. Applied too early, the sugar burns. Applied at the end, it caramelizes into a sticky, lacquered finish.

**Ingredients**

- 1 cup ketchup
- 1/2 cup tomato sauce or crushed tomatoes
- 1/2 cup brown sugar
- 1/4 cup molasses
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 2 tbsp yellow mustard
- 2 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
- 1 tsp smoked paprika
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp onion powder
- 1 tsp chili powder
- 1/2 tsp black pepper
- 1/2 tsp kosher salt
- 1/4 tsp cayenne (optional, for a little bite)

**Steps**

1. Whisk all ingredients together in a saucepan until smooth.
2. Bring to a gentle boil over medium heat, then immediately reduce to low.
3. Simmer 20 to 30 minutes, stirring often, until thick, glossy, and pourable.
4. Taste and adjust: more molasses or brown sugar for sweeter, more vinegar for tang, more cayenne for heat.
5. Cool completely before storing. Use as a finishing glaze in the last 10 to 20 minutes on ribs, chicken, or pork.

**Do's**
- Apply as a glaze only in the final stage of cooking so the sugars caramelize without burning.
- Let the sauce cool and thicken before judging the consistency; it sets up as it cools.

**Don'ts**
- Don't brush it on at the start of a long cook. The sugar will scorch.
- Don't skip the molasses; it is what separates KC sauce from generic sweet BBQ sauce.

## Eastern Carolina Vinegar Sauce

Eastern Carolina sauce is the polar opposite of Kansas City. No tomato, no sugar to speak of, no thickness. It is thin, sharp, and peppery. The whole point is to cut through the richness of pulled pork and let the smoke flavor come through. You either drench the pulled pork directly or serve it at the table in a squeeze bottle.

**Ingredients**

- 1 cup distilled white vinegar
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 1 tbsp brown sugar (just enough to take the raw edge off)
- 1 tbsp red pepper flakes
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp black pepper
- 1/2 tsp cayenne
- Optional: 1 tsp hot sauce (Texas Pete or Crystal)

**Steps**

1. Combine all ingredients in a jar or bowl and whisk until the sugar and salt dissolve.
2. Let it sit at room temperature for at least 1 hour (overnight is better) so the pepper flakes bloom.
3. Taste: it should be sharp, peppery, and slightly sweet. Adjust vinegar ratio, salt, or heat as needed.
4. Shake well before every use. It will separate and that is normal.

**Do's**
- Shake it every time. The pepper flakes settle.
- Use it liberally on pulled pork; the thin consistency soaks in rather than sitting on top.
- Make it a day ahead for the best flavor.

**Don'ts**
- Don't add tomato. That makes it a Western Carolina sauce (different thing entirely).
- Don't expect it to look like "BBQ sauce." It is thin, it is supposed to be thin.

## South Carolina Mustard Sauce (Carolina Gold)

South Carolina mustard sauce is built on yellow mustard, sweetened with honey and brown sugar, sharpened with vinegar. It is the classic pairing for pulled pork in the Midlands of South Carolina, and it works surprisingly well on chicken and smoked sausage. The color is unmistakable.

**Ingredients**

- 3/4 cup yellow mustard
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 1/3 cup honey
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 2 tbsp ketchup
- 1 to 2 tsp Worcestershire sauce
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1/2 tsp black pepper
- 1/2 tsp kosher salt
- Pinch of cayenne or a dash of hot sauce (optional)

**Steps**

1. Whisk all ingredients together in a bowl until completely smooth.
2. Taste and adjust: more honey or brown sugar for sweetness, more vinegar for tang.
3. For a mellower flavor, simmer on low heat for 5 to 10 minutes then cool. Or use it straight as a no-cook sauce.
4. Store in the fridge. It keeps 1 to 2 weeks easily thanks to the mustard and vinegar acting as natural preservatives.

**Do's**
- Try it on pulled pork first. That is the canonical pairing.
- Use it as a table sauce; it does not need to be cooked onto the meat.

**Don'ts**
- Don't use Dijon or whole-grain mustard. Yellow mustard is the foundation of Carolina Gold.
- Don't overdo the ketchup. It is there for color and a touch of tomato sweetness, not as a co-star.

## Alabama White Sauce

Alabama white sauce was invented at Big Bob Gibson Bar-B-Q in Decatur, Alabama, in 1925. It is mayonnaise-based, tangy, peppery, and specifically designed for smoked chicken. The traditional move is to dunk an entire smoked chicken half into a vat of white sauce, but it works just as well served on the side.

**Ingredients**

- 1 cup mayonnaise
- 1/4 cup apple cider vinegar
- 2 to 3 tbsp lemon juice
- 1 to 2 tbsp prepared horseradish
- 1 tbsp sugar or honey
- 1 tsp black pepper (freshly ground)
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- Pinch of cayenne or a splash of hot sauce (optional)
- 1 to 3 tbsp water to thin as needed

**Steps**

1. Whisk together mayo, vinegar, lemon juice, horseradish, sugar, pepper, garlic, and salt until smooth.
2. Add water a tablespoon at a time until it is pourable but still coats a spoon.
3. Taste and adjust: more vinegar for sharper tang, more sugar for balance, more horseradish for bite.
4. Chill at least 30 minutes before serving so the flavors meld. Keeps refrigerated about 1 week.

**Do's**
- Make it at least 30 minutes ahead; it improves as the flavors come together.
- Try it as a dipping sauce for smoked chicken thighs, wings, or drumsticks.

**Don'ts**
- Don't skip the horseradish. It provides the subtle heat that distinguishes white sauce from flavored mayo.
- Don't use it as a cooking glaze. The mayo will break at high heat. This is a finishing or table sauce.

## Texas Mop Sauce

Texas mop sauce is thin, savory, and barely sweet. It is meant to be applied during cooking with a cotton mop or brush, keeping the surface moist and building layers of flavor over a long smoke. The name is literal. In Texas competition and restaurant pits, they mop briskets with this throughout the cook.

**Ingredients**

- 1 cup beef stock or water
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 1/4 cup Worcestershire sauce
- 2 tbsp yellow or Dijon mustard
- 2 tbsp tomato paste
- 1 tbsp brown sugar
- 1 tsp chili powder
- 1 tsp smoked paprika
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp onion powder
- 1/2 tsp black pepper
- 1/2 tsp kosher salt
- 1 to 2 tbsp butter or tallow (optional, for richness)

**Steps**

1. Combine all ingredients in a small saucepan and whisk until smooth.
2. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat.
3. Reduce to low and simmer 15 to 20 minutes, stirring occasionally, until slightly thickened.
4. If using butter or tallow, stir it in at the end.
5. Keep warm while mopping. Apply with a mop or brush every 45 to 60 minutes during long cooks.
6. Cool any leftovers and store in the fridge for up to 2 weeks.

**Do's**
- Keep the mop sauce warm in a pot next to the pit so it does not cool down the meat surface.
- Apply it thin and often rather than thick and once.

**Don'ts**
- Don't confuse this with a finishing sauce. It is applied during cooking, not at the table.
- Don't mop in the first 2 hours of a brisket cook; let the bark set first.

## Western Carolina (Lexington) Red Dip

Western Carolina sauce, sometimes called "Lexington dip," splits the difference between Eastern Carolina vinegar and Kansas City tomato. It is vinegar-based but with enough ketchup to give it a reddish tint and a touch of sweetness. It is thinner than KC sauce and tangier, designed for sliced pork shoulder.

**Ingredients**

- 1 cup apple cider vinegar
- 1/2 cup ketchup
- 1/4 cup brown sugar
- 2 tbsp yellow mustard
- 2 to 3 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
- 1 tsp onion powder
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1/2 tsp black pepper
- 1/2 tsp cayenne (or less, to taste)
- 1/2 tsp kosher salt

**Steps**

1. Combine all ingredients in a saucepan and whisk smooth.
2. Bring just to a simmer, then reduce to low and cook 10 to 15 minutes, stirring occasionally.
3. Taste: add more vinegar for sharper tang or more sugar for balance.
4. Cool and store refrigerated. Use as a table sauce or light finishing glaze on sliced pork.

**Do's**
- Serve it alongside sliced pork shoulder. That is the traditional use.
- Think of it as a bridge between East and West: vinegar bite with a little tomato warmth.

**Don'ts**
- Don't thicken it into a Kansas City consistency. It should stay loose and pourable.
- Don't skip the mustard. It adds complexity even though you cannot taste it distinctly.

## Sweet Honey-Garlic Sauce

This is not region-specific. It is a backyard-friendly, sweet sauce that works on chicken wings, pork chops, grilled shrimp, and anything else that benefits from a sticky, garlicky glaze. It caramelizes beautifully in the last few minutes on the grill or smoker.

**Ingredients**

- 1 cup ketchup or tomato sauce
- 1/3 cup honey
- 1/4 cup brown sugar
- 1/4 cup apple cider vinegar
- 2 tbsp soy sauce (for umami) or Worcestershire
- 3 to 4 cloves garlic, finely minced (or 1-1/2 tsp garlic powder)
- 1 tsp onion powder
- 1/2 tsp black pepper
- 1/2 tsp kosher salt
- Optional: pinch of chili flakes or hot sauce

**Steps**

1. If using fresh garlic, saute it in a teaspoon of oil over medium heat for 30 to 60 seconds until fragrant. Skip this step if using garlic powder.
2. Add all remaining ingredients to the pan and whisk until combined.
3. Simmer gently for 10 to 15 minutes, stirring, until slightly thickened and glossy.
4. Taste: add more honey if you want it sweeter, more vinegar if it feels flat.
5. Cool and store in the fridge. Use as a glaze in the last 5 to 10 minutes on the grill so the honey does not scorch.

**Do's**
- Apply late in the cook. Honey burns fast.
- Double the recipe if you want to use some for glazing and some for dipping.

**Don'ts**
- Don't simmer longer than 15 minutes or it will reduce past "glaze" into "candy."
- Don't skip the soy sauce or Worcestershire. The umami is what keeps it from tasting like pancake syrup.

## Pairing Guide

Different sauces work better with different meats and cooking styles. Here is a starting point:

- **Brisket:** Texas mop (during cook), Western Carolina red dip (at the table), or KC style (as a finishing glaze).
- **Pulled pork:** Eastern Carolina vinegar, South Carolina mustard, or Western Carolina red dip.
- **Ribs:** Kansas City (glazed in the last 20 minutes), honey-garlic (last 10 minutes), or Alabama white (on the side).
- **Smoked chicken:** Alabama white sauce is the canonical choice. Honey-garlic and KC style also work well as glazes.
- **Pork chops and sausage:** Carolina Gold mustard, honey-garlic, or Western Carolina dip.
- **Fish:** Alabama white sauce or a squeeze of the Carolina vinegar, lightly applied.

***

## Storage Notes

- All of these sauces store well in sealed glass jars in the refrigerator for 1 to 2 weeks.
- Vinegar-based sauces (Eastern Carolina, Western Carolina, mop) last the longest due to high acidity.
- Mayo-based sauces (Alabama white) have the shortest shelf life; use within a week.
- For longer preservation beyond 2 weeks, follow proper canning and food safety guidelines.
- Bring any refrigerated sauce to room temperature (or warm it gently) before serving for the best flavor.
