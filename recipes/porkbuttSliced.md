**step‑by‑step, offset‑specific** plan for **pork butt sliced shoulder (3–4 lb Boston butt)** on your Royal Gourmet CC1830S, tuned for juicy, flavorful slices rather than pulled pork.  

***

## 1. Choose and prep the pork butt (sliced)

- **Target size:** 3–4 lb Boston butt, preferably with a decent fat cap (1/4–3/8").  
- **Trimming:**  
  - Remove big ragged edges, but keep the fat cap and internal marbling.  

**Do’s**  
- Trim neatly and pat dry before seasoning.  
- Apply a **moderate rub** (salt, pepper, garlic powder, onion powder, mild paprika, pinch of sugar) 30–60 minutes before you light the coals. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t over‑trim or slice off the fat cap; you want that to keep the meat moist.  
- Don’t oversalt if you also plan to baste later.  

***

## 2. Set up the Royal Gourmet offset smoker

- **Target temp:** 225–250°F for a small butt going to be sliced.  
- **Fuel:**  
  - Charcoal basket in the firebox with a **minion‑style fire** (2/3 unlit, 1/3 lit) plus 1–2 small wood chunks (apple, hickory, maple). [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Use **baffle / tuning plates** so the heat travels under the grate and isn’t blasting one side. [best practice]  
- Place a **water pan** on the grate on the stack side to stabilize temps. [best practice]  
- Keep the **exhaust stack mostly open**, and fine‑tune with the **firebox intake**. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t over‑fire the firebox with whole logs.  
- Don’t open the lid during warm‑up or the first part of the cook.  

***

## 3. Place the butt on the offset

- **Position:** Stack side of the chamber, not right next to the firebox.  
- **Orientation:**  
  - You can go **fat‑side up** for a more forgiving bark and slightly juicier slices, or **fat‑side down** if you want a bit more crust on the presentation side. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Insert a **meat‑probe thermometer** into the thickest part, avoiding large fat pockets.  
- Insert a **grate‑level probe** near the butt to monitor pit temp. [best practice]  
- Close the lid and **do not open it for 1.5–2 hours** to let the bark form and the heat stabilize. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t put the butt directly on the firebox side.  
- Don’t move it around frequently; let it cook undisturbed.  

***

## 4. Spritz and bark development (Hours 2–3)

After 2 hours, open the lid **quickly** and:

- **Spritz** every 30–45 minutes with a 50/50 mix of **apple juice and water** or **beef broth and water** to keep the surface a bit moist.  
- Watch for the **bark to set**: dark, tacky, not powdery or bright‑pink.  

For a **sliced shoulder**, you want a **thick, darker bark** on the exposed side that will look appetizing when you slice.

**Do’s**  
- Let the bark set before thinking about wrapping; a small sliced butt usually does **not** need to be wrapped unless you’re worried about drying out. [best practice]  
- If you **do** wrap (for bad weather, long cooks, or a very lean piece), wait until the bark is fully developed and then wrap tightly in **butcher paper** with a light splash of liquid. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t wrap too early; you’ll lose bark and color.  
- Don’t spritz too aggressively; you want a light mist, not a deluge.  

***

## 5. Time and temp for 3–4 lb sliced pork butt at 225–250°F

**Goal:** 195–205°F internal, **probe‑tender** but not mushy (you’re slicing, not shredding). [best practice]  

| Situation | Time |
| --- | --- |
| Naked only (ideal for slicing) | Cook uncovered the whole time, 4–5.5 hours total. |  
| Naked → wrapped at 170–175°F | 2–3 hours naked, 2–3 hours wrapped. |

**Target internal temp:** 195–203°F; **probe‑tender but still holding its shape** when you press the meat. [best practice]  

**Do’s**  
- Probe at 180–185°F, then 190°F, then 195°F, checking tenderness.  
- Keep the **stack mostly open** and adjust the **firebox intake** in small steps. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t pull it at 190°F if it’s still firm.  
- Don’t go past 205°F if you’re aiming for nice slices; past that, it starts to shred.  

***

## 6. Minute‑by‑minute cook script (3–4 lb sliced pork butt)

Use this as a mental guide; adjust for your butt’s behavior.

| Time since fire‑up | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 0–30 min | Fire up, light chimney, build minion‑fire, let pit stabilize at 225–250°F. [best practice] |
| 30–60 min | Put the butt on the smoker, fat‑side up or down, stack side. Close the lid. [best practice] |
| 2 hours | Quick spritz; check bark color. If it’s still light, spritz every 30–45 min. [best practice] |
| 2.5–3.5 hours | Check bark and temp.  
  - If bark is dark, set, and temp is 170–175°F, you can keep it naked or wrap lightly if you want to finish faster. [best practice] |
| 3.5–5.5 hours | Continue cooking, only opening the lid to check the probe at 180°F, 190°F, 195°F, and then 200°F. [best practice] |

**Target finish:** 195–203°F internal, **probe‑tender** but still slice‑able.  

***

## 7. Resting and slicing

**Do’s**  
- Pull when it’s **195–203°F and probe‑tender**, then **keep it whole, wrapped in foil or butcher paper**, and rest **at least 1.5–2 hours** on the counter. [best practice]  
- After 1.5–2 hours, unwrap and let it sit 15–30 minutes to firm up slightly.  
- Slice **against the grain** in 1/4–1/2 inch thick slices; thicker slices hold more juice and texture. [best practice]  
- Serve with a light drizzle of the collected juices or a little dipping sauce on the side. [best practice]  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t slice immediately off the smoker; it will be tough and dry.  
- Don’t slice too thin; you’ll lose the juicy, meaty bite.  
- Don’t over‑pull or shred it; you’re going for slices, not pulled pork.  

***

## Quick “Do’s / Don’ts” for sliced pork butt on your offset

**Do’s**  
- Use a moderate rub and let it sit 30–60 minutes before lighting.  
- Keep the smoker sealed (doors, seams), use baffle/tuning plates, and manage temps via the firebox intake. [best practice]  
- Keep it mostly **naked**, unless you’re worried about drying out, to maximize bark and slicing texture.  

**Don’ts**  
- Don’t over‑smoke; keep wood chunks modest and stop active smoke once the bark is set.  
- Don’t open the lid constantly to check on the butt.  
- Don’t skip the rest; it’s critical for juicy, sliceable meat.
