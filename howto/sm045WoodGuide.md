# SM045 Wood Guide

How much wood to use in the SM045, which species pair with which proteins, and the most common over-smoking mistakes.

---

## Why wood amounts matter more on the SM045

The SM045 is a low-airflow, sealed electric cabinet. On a traditional offset smoker, smoke flows through the chamber and exits the stack — a continuous draft dilutes and refreshes the smoke environment. On the SM045, smoke builds up inside the cabinet and stays there.

This means:

- **Less wood produces equivalent or stronger smoke flavor** compared to an offset
- **Over-smoking happens fast** — one extra oz can push a cook from pleasant to harsh
- **Wood species choice matters more** — aggressive woods like mesquite and heavy hickory compound fast in an enclosed space

The manufacturer baseline is **1–2 oz of hardwood chunks per 60 lb of product**. For typical home loads of 5–7 lb, that translates to **1–2 oz total for the entire cook**. This feels like very little if you're coming from offset cooking. Trust it.

---

## How to measure wood

Buy a kitchen scale. "One chunk" is not a measurement — chunks vary wildly in size. Weigh your wood every time until you develop an instinct for what 1 oz and 2 oz look like.

| Weight | Approximate chunk size |
| --- | --- |
| 0.5 oz | Roughly a large marble |
| 1 oz | Roughly a golf ball |
| 2 oz | Two golf balls, or one fist-sized piece |

Cut larger chunks down with a hatchet or saw to reach the right weight. Pieces should be roughly 1–2" — small enough to sit fully in the wood box without blocking the lid.

## Wood species guide

### Strong smoke woods — use sparingly in the SM045

| Species | Flavor profile | SM045 max |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hickory | Classic BBQ, savory, strong | 2 oz for beef/pork; 1 oz for poultry |
| Mesquite | Intense, earthy, can be harsh | 0.5–1 oz maximum; not for long cooks |
| Oak | Clean, medium, versatile | 1.5–2 oz for beef; 1 oz for lighter proteins |

### Mild smoke woods — more forgiving

| Species | Flavor profile | SM045 use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Apple | Mild, slightly sweet | 1–1.5 oz for pork/poultry; 0.5–1 oz for seafood |
| Cherry | Mild, slightly fruity, good color | 1 oz for poultry/pork; 0.5 oz for seafood |
| Pecan | Nutty, medium, versatile | 1–2 oz for beef/pork |
| Maple | Light, slightly sweet | 1–1.5 oz for bacon, pork belly, poultry |
| Alder | Very mild, almost neutral | 0.5–1 oz for salmon, delicate seafood |

## Wood amounts by protein

These are the amounts to load at the start of the cook. Load once and don't add more.

| Protein | Wood amount | Recommended species |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Brisket (sliced or pulled) | 2 oz | Hickory, oak, or pecan |
| Pastrami | 2 oz | Hickory or apple |
| Beef chuck roast (smoke phase) | 1–2 oz | Oak or hickory |
| Beef cheeks (smoke phase) | 1–2 oz | Oak or hickory |
| Tri-tip | 1 oz | Oak or hickory |
| Pork butt (pulled or sliced) | 2 oz | Hickory or apple |
| Spare ribs | 1–2 oz | Apple or hickory |
| Baby back ribs | 1–2 oz | Apple or hickory |
| Pork belly / bacon | 1–2 oz | Apple or maple |
| Whole chicken (spatchcocked) | 1 oz | Apple or cherry |
| Chicken thighs | 1 oz | Apple or cherry |
| Chicken wings | 1 oz | Apple or cherry |
| Salmon | 1 oz | Apple or alder |
| Swordfish | 1 oz | Apple or maple |
| Lobster tails | 1 oz | Apple or cherry |
| Vegetables | 0.5–1 oz | Apple or oak |

## Mixing wood species

You can mix species in the wood box. Common combinations:

- **Hickory + apple (50/50):** Rounds out hickory's sharp edges on pork; classic competition BBQ flavor
- **Oak + cherry:** Deepens the smoke color and adds subtle sweetness to beef
- **Apple + maple:** Very mild, sweet combination for bacon and delicate proteins

Keep the total weight within the recommended range regardless of species.

## Common over-smoking mistakes on the SM045

### Mistake 1 — Using chip-sized pieces

Wood chips burn fast, spike smoke production briefly, then go inert. The SM045 needs a slow, sustained smolder. Use chunks, not chips.

### Mistake 2 — Adding wood mid-cook

The wood box empties and the temptation is to open the door and reload. Don't. The initial charge is calibrated for the whole cook. More wood mid-cook adds harsh late-stage smoke to already-cooked meat. If the wood runs out and you're still in the early window (first 2 hours), a brief reload is acceptable. After that, leave it.

### Mistake 3 — Using offset quantities

If you're used to adding a wood split every 45 minutes on an offset, you may instinctively want 4–6 oz in the SM045. That will produce acrid, over-smoked meat. The low airflow environment is fundamentally different — start with 2 oz and evaluate after your first cook.

### Mistake 4 — Using mesquite on long cooks

Mesquite is intense and becomes increasingly bitter over a long, enclosed cook. Reserve it for short cooks (under 2 hours) on beef only, and cap it at 1 oz.

### Mistake 5 — Wet wood or pre-soaking

Do not soak wood before using it in the SM045. Wet wood produces steam and white acrid smoke in an enclosed cabinet. Dry chunks smolder cleanly.

## Signs of good smoke vs. over-smoking

### Good smoke (what you want)
- Thin, barely visible haze inside the cabinet when you briefly open the door
- Meat has a warm reddish-brown smoke ring or color
- Flavor is savory, smoky, and clean — not bitter

### Over-smoking (what to avoid)
- Heavy gray or white smoke visible when you open the door
- Meat has a gray, sooty exterior
- Flavor is bitter, harsh, or medicinal
- Strong smoke smell that lingers long after eating

If your last SM045 cook tasted bitter or harsh, reduce wood by 25–50% on the next cook and compare.

## Wood that does NOT work in the SM045

Avoid these entirely:

| Material | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Softwoods (pine, cedar, spruce) | Contains resins that produce toxic, acrid smoke |
| Treated or painted wood | Contains chemicals; never use in any smoker |
| Wood chips (short-term option only) | Burn too fast; not calibrated for SM045 cooks |
| Pellets | Designed for pellet grill auger systems; can smother in the SM045 wood box |
| Green (freshly cut) wood | Too much moisture; produces harsh white smoke |
