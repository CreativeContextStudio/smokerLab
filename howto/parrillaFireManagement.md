Mastering fire on the parrilla is what separates an asador from someone who simply puts meat on a grill. The entire system \u2014 brasero, ember bed, grate height \u2014 is a precision heat-management tool. Understanding it changes the quality of every cook.

***

## El Brasero / The Ember-Maker

The **brasero** is the fire-making zone of the parrilla \u2014 a separate area beside or adjacent to the cooking grate where hardwood logs burn down to coals. The asador does not cook over the living fire. The fire is in the brasero; only settled, glowing embers go under the grate.

**Why this matters:** A wood fire produces inconsistent, intensely hot flames that scorch the exterior of meat before the interior cooks. Embers produce radiant, even, penetrating heat \u2014 the kind that renders fat slowly, develops deep colour without burning, and fills the meat with flavour.

### Building the brasero fire

1. **Stack 3\u20134 hardwood logs** in a cross-pattern (log-cabin style) in the brasero. The cross-stack creates airflow channels that help the fire establish quickly.
2. **Light with newspaper or a natural fire starter** \u2014 never lighter fluid. Place the starter under the stack and light at two or three points.
3. **Do not rush this phase.** Let the fire burn freely, adding one more log every 15\u201320 minutes as the fire establishes. The goal is to get all the wood burning vigorously.
4. **The fire will take 45\u201360 minutes** to burn down to usable coals. Use this time to prep the meat, prepare sides, and receive guests.

***

## Lectura de Brasas / Reading the Embers

The colour and texture of your embers tells you everything about heat level:

| Ember Appearance | Heat Level | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Orange-red with active glowing | Very hot | Not ready \u2014 too active, still producing flame |
| Deep orange-red, no visible flame | High | Searing (entra\u00F1a, matambre at low grate) |
| Orange with white-grey ash coating | Medium-high | General asado (most beef cuts at mid grate) |
| White-grey throughout, orange glow beneath | Medium | Slow cooking (asado de tira, bondiola at high grate) |
| Mostly grey, fading | Low/dying | Need to rake fresh coals or add more to brasero |

**The ideal working ember bed for most of the asado** is the third state: orange with white-grey ash coating. This means the coals are fully established, there is no risk of active flame, and the radiant heat is steady and controlled.

## Raking and Distributing Embers

The asador\u2019s main job during a cook is managing the ember bed under the grate.

### Initial coal bed

Once the brasero has produced a generous pile of orange-grey coals (45\u201360 minutes after lighting), rake a **generous, even layer** under the entire grate. You want:
- Uniform depth across the full grate area
- No thin spots that will create cold zones
- A depth of 4\u20136 cm of settled coals

### Maintaining heat during the cook

A typical 2\u20133 hour asado will exhaust the initial ember bed. The brasero must continue burning throughout to supply fresh coals.

**Every 20\u201330 minutes:**
1. Add one fresh hardwood log to the brasero to keep it producing coals.
2. When the coals under the grate begin to grey and lose heat (the cook will slow and the sizzle will quiet), rake fresh coals from the brasero to replenish the cooking zone.

**Listen to the grate:** A steady, active sizzle means the heat is right. A quiet or dying sizzle means you need fresh coals underneath.

## Altura de la Parrilla / Grate Height Control

The grate\u2019s height above the ember bed is the primary heat-intensity control on a parrilla. Learn to use it instinctively.

### High grate (15\u201320 cm / 6\u20138 inches above embers)
- **Gentle, steady heat**
- For: asado de tira, bondiola, pamplona \u2014 anything that needs 60\u201390+ minutes on the fire
- Effect: renders fat slowly, cooks through without charring, allows the long times these cuts need

### Mid grate (8\u201315 cm / 3\u20136 inches above embers)
- **Medium heat**
- For: pollo, matambre, chorizo \u2014 cuts that need 15\u201360 minutes
- Effect: produces browning and crust without burning; the workhorse position

### Low grate (5\u20138 cm / 2\u20133 inches above embers)
- **Intense, searing heat**
- For: entra\u00F1a, morrones (charring), bread warming
- Effect: aggressive sear, rapid char \u2014 use only for short-duration tasks

### Adjusting on the fly

- **Meat is charring too fast:** Raise the grate immediately. Do not remove the meat unless necessary.
- **Meat is cooking too slowly or sizzle has gone quiet:** Rake fresh coals underneath and/or lower the grate one position.
- **Active flame flare-up:** Raise the grate immediately. Flare-ups from fat dripping onto active embers are normal; raising the grate removes the meat from the flame while the flare dies.

***

## Zonas de Calor / Heat Zones

A parrilla is not a single-temperature surface. Use different zones deliberately:

- **Main zone (over the densest coals):** Primary cooking area for the main cuts.
- **Edge zone (cooler perimeter, less coal coverage):** For morcilla, achuras, and anything that needs gentle heat. Also good for warming bread.
- **Resting zone (off the direct coal area):** A corner of the grate with no coals beneath \u2014 for holding cooked items warm without overcooking.

An experienced asador always has all three zones in use simultaneously: something searing on the main zone, something warming on the edge zone, and something resting in the rest zone.

***

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Light the brasero 60\u201390 minutes before cooking \u2014 no shortcuts.
- Let the fire burn fully to white-grey/orange coals before raking under the grate.
- Keep the brasero producing fresh coals throughout the entire cook.
- Listen to the sizzle and adjust coal depth or grate height accordingly.
- Use high grate for slow cuts, low grate for fast sears.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t rake flames under the grate \u2014 wait for embers.
- Don\u2019t let the brasero go cold halfway through the cook.
- Don\u2019t cook everything at one grate height \u2014 different cuts need different heat.
- Don\u2019t ignore a quieting sizzle \u2014 it means the coal bed is dying and needs attention.
- Don\u2019t use lighter fluid or accelerants to restart a fading brasero \u2014 add fresh wood and give it time.
