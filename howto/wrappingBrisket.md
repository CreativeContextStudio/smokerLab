Wrapping a **small brisket** on an offset like your **Royal Gourmet CC1830S** is about **preserving bark, not killing it** while speeding up the cook. Here are concrete, offset‑specific tips.

***

## 1. When to wrap (small brisket)

- **Primary signal:**  
  - Dark, tacky, “set” bark; the rub is fixed to the surface and not powdery anymore. This usually happens around **165–180°F internal**, but go by look/feel, not just the clock. [meatchurch](https://www.meatchurch.com/blogs/recipes/brisket)

- **Small brisket shortcut:**  
  - At 250–275°F, small pieces (3–5 lb) can reach wrapping stage **after roughly 2.5–4 hours**, depending on thickness. [facebook](https://www.facebook.com/groups/northtexasbbq/posts/8972702009503111/)

- **If bark is too dark:**  
  - Wrap earlier, even if internal temp is a bit lower, so you don’t push the bark into bitter or leathery. [eggheadforum](https://eggheadforum.com/discussion/1230106/brisket-wrapped-timing)

- **If bark is still light:**  
  - Keep it naked longer; thin, washed‑out bark cannot be “fixed” by wrapping. [girlscangrill](https://girlscangrill.com/bbq-tips/how-wrapping-brisket-affects-bark/)

***

## 2. What to wrap with (paper vs foil on an offset)

- **Butcher paper (unwaxed):**  
  - Best for a **small offset cooked at 250–275°F**.  
  - Protects the bark, still lets a little smoke and moisture through, and keeps the bark crisper than foil. [smokedbbqsource](https://www.smokedbbqsource.com/butcher-paper-vs-foil/)

- **Aluminum foil (“Texas crutch”):**  
  - Faster cook, very juicy, but will soften the bark.  
  - Better if you’re short on time, cooking leaner meat, or want comfort‑food‑style brisket. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O86otwJHsvo)

For your small offset, **peach butcher paper** is the ideal “first‑cook” choice; foil is fine too if you accept softer bark. [meatchurch](https://www.meatchurch.com/blogs/recipes/brisket)

***

## 3. How to wrap on the smoker

1. **Put out a small fire‑out tray** (heavy foil or tray) on the grate so you can lay the brisket on it without making a mess.  
2. **Lay down a big sheet of butcher paper or foil** (or 2–3 sheets if you’re paranoid about leaks).  
3. **Optional:**  
   - Lightly spray the inside of the paper with a little water or beef broth so it’s more pliable and less likely to tear. [cusocuts](https://cusocuts.com/blogs/recipes/when-to-wrap-a-brisket)
4. **Place the brisket fat‑side up** on the paper/foil.  
5. **Fold the sides up and over the meat**, then roll the brisket while tightening the wrap so it’s snug but not suffocating.  
6. **Use the Drag & Fold method (Royal Gourmet‑style):**  
   - Pull the paper/foil under the brisket while rolling it forward, using the meat itself to tighten the wrap. [traeger](https://www.traeger.com/learn/how-to-wrap-a-brisket)
7. **Tuck in the ends** so no big gaps leak steam or oil.  

On the offset, keep the lid closed as much as possible; only open long enough to load the brisket back in once wrapped. [cusocuts](https://cusocuts.com/blogs/recipes/when-to-wrap-a-brisket)

***

## 4. Where to put the wrapped brisket in the offset

- **Place the wrapped brisket on the side opposite the firebox** (stack side), where the heat is gentler. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SFiZKjhjzsg)
- **Avoid the hot spot right next to the firebox door**; that’s where the paper/foil can split and the meat can come apart. [bbqspitrotisseries.com](https://www.bbqspitrotisseries.com.au/blog/maintaining-steady-temperatures-in-an-offset-smoke)
- **If you have a drip pan**, keep it under or near the wrapped brisket to catch juices and add a bit of humidity. [urbancowgirllife](https://urbancowgirllife.com/how-long-to-cook-a-brisket-at-250/)

***

## 5. What changes after wrapping

- **Wrap = faster cook:**  
  - Once wrapped, the brisket usually takes **1–2 hours longer** to finish compared to when it hit 165–180°F, depending on size and smoker temp. [smokingmeatforums](https://www.smokingmeatforums.com/threads/small-brisket-flat-questions.158999/)
  - Small briskets at 250–275°F may go from 180°F internal to 200°F in **2–4 hours** once wrapped. [facebook](https://www.facebook.com/groups/northtexasbbq/posts/8972702009503111/)

- **Adjust vents slightly:**  
  - The wrapped brisket acts like an insulator, so temps can creep up unless you slightly **close the firebox intake** (by a small notch) after wrapping. [thebarbecuelab](https://thebarbecuelab.com/offset-smoker-fire-management/)

- **Probe tenderness, not just temp:**  
  - Target **198–205°F internal**, or **probe‑tender** (slides in like room‑temperature butter). [smokejustis](https://www.smokejustis.com/smokes-blog/avoid-these-4-mistakes-when-smoking-brisket)
  - Small briskets can stall less obviously, so probing is critical. [girlscangrill](https://girlscangrill.com/bbq-tips/how-to-fix-undercooked-brisket/)

***

## 6. Avoiding common wrap mistakes on the offset

- **Mistake:** Wrapping too early (bark not set).  
  - Result: mushy, indistinct bark and over‑smoked meat.  
  - Fix: Wait until the rub is locked in and the color you like has formed. [eggheadforum](https://eggheadforum.com/discussion/1230106/brisket-wrapped-timing)

- **Mistake:** Wrapping too late (bark already hard/brittle).  
  - Fix: Wrap while the bark is still a bit tacky, not wood‑like. [girlscangrill](https://girlscangrill.com/bbq-tips/how-wrapping-brisket-affects-bark/)

- **Mistake:** Over‑boiling the meat in the wrap (lots of liquid in the foil).  
  - Fix: Use a light spritz or a smear of butter on the meat before wrapping; keep extra liquids in the smoker low. [butcherbbq](https://butcherbbq.com/blogs/news/unlock-the-secrets-of-brisket-wrapping-liquids)

- **Mistake:** Opening the lid constantly after wrapping because you’re worried about the wrap failing.  
  - Fix: Trust the wrap; check only when you need to (adjust vents, probe, or spritz the outside before the second half). [bbqspitrotisseries.com](https://www.bbqspitrotisseries.com.au/blog/maintaining-steady-temperatures-in-an-offset-smoke)

***

## Simple small-brisket wrap‑script for your Royal Gourmet

On a 3–5 lb piece at 250–275°F on your CC1830S:

1. **Let bark develop** 2.5–4 hours until it’s dark and set (165–180°F).  
2. **Wrap tight in butcher paper** using the drag‑and‑fold method, add a little light broth on the inside of the paper if you like. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/EwnXiXY1G-c)
3. **Put it on the stack side**, keep the exhaust mostly open, and slightly close the firebox intake if temps rise.  
4. **Let it cook another 2–4 hours** until **198–205°F and probe‑tender**.  
5. **Rest wrapped 1–2 hours**, then slice. [smokingmeatforums](https://www.smokingmeatforums.com/threads/small-brisket-flat-questions.158999/)
