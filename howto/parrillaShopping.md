A proper Uruguayan asado starts long before the fire \u2014 it starts with the right fuel, the right tools, and a parrilla that is ready. Here is everything you need to set up for a full traditional asado.

***

## Combustible / Hardwood Fuel

The soul of the parrilla is hardwood, not charcoal briquettes. The distinction matters enormously over a long cook.

**What to look for:**

- **Quebracho** (Argentina/Uruguay): The gold standard. Dense, hot-burning, long-lasting coals. Burns slowly and produces a mineral-clean smoke.
- **Eucalyptus** (Uruguay\u2019s most common fuel): Widely available, very hot, produces excellent coals. A slight herbal note in the smoke.
- **Oak** (widely available in the USA and Europe): Excellent substitute. Clean-burning, good coal production, neutral flavour. A fine option when quebracho or eucalyptus is unavailable.
- **Fruit woods (apple, cherry, peach):** Good supplementary wood for a pleasant, mild smoke character. Too soft to be primary fuel \u2014 burns too fast.

**How much to buy:**

- For a full Sunday asado (4\u20136 guests, 2\u20133 hours of cooking): **10\u201315 lb of hardwood logs.**
- Buy splits or rounds 20\u201330 cm (8\u201312 inches) long, roughly wrist-thick. They are easier to manage than full logs.

**What to avoid:**
- Softwood (pine, cedar, fir): Resinous, acrid smoke, low-quality coals.
- Charcoal briquettes as primary fuel: Acceptable as a supplement or to start the fire faster, but they do not produce the same radiant ember quality as hardwood.
- Any wood treated with chemicals or paint.

**Natural fire starters** (no lighter fluid): Newspaper, dried kindling, or natural wax fire cubes. Lighter fluid leaves a chemical taste in the early coals.

## Herramientas Esenciales / Essential Tools

### Long-handled tongs (16\u201318 inches)
The most-used tool on the parrilla. Long handles keep your hands away from the heat. Buy one pair of heavy, spring-loaded tongs. Avoid the thin, flexible variety \u2014 they have no grip strength on heavy cuts.

### Wide flat spatula or asador fork
For turning bondiola, pamplona, and morcilla \u2014 items that need more support than tongs can provide.

### Heat-resistant gloves
For adjusting the grate height and for reaching into the brasero with a shovel. Welding gloves work well and are inexpensive.

### Wire grill brush
For cleaning the grate before cooking and between uses. The grate is cleaned hot, not cold \u2014 brush it immediately after the cook while it is still warm.

### Metal shovel or poker
For moving embers from the brasero under the grate, for adjusting coal depth, and for excavating papas al rescoldo. A standard fireplace or garden shovel is fine. Avoid wooden handles.

### Sal gruesa
Coarse sea salt or kosher salt in a large, open container. The parrillero seasons with their hands, not with a shaker. You need to be able to grab a generous pinch and apply it from above. Buy a 1 lb bag; you will use more than you expect.

***

## Opcionales Recomendados / Recommended Additions

### Cast-iron disco (plow disc, 18\u201322 inches)
For pollo al disco, stews, and various campfire dishes. The disco is placed over coals, not on the grate. It is not essential for a traditional asado but opens up a whole category of parrilla cooking.

### Cast-iron skillet
For provoleta (grilled provolone) \u2014 a cast-iron skillet placed on the grate gives more control than putting cheese directly on the bars.

### Butcher\u2019s twine
For tying pamplona (stuffed beef roll). A roll of natural cotton butcher\u2019s twine is a small investment that is used repeatedly.

### Instant-read thermometer
Not traditional, but useful for poultry and bondiola when you are still learning to read doneness by feel and sound alone.

## Configuraci\u00F3n de la Parrilla / Parrilla Setup Notes

A traditional Uruguayan parrilla has two zones:
- **Brasero:** The fire-making area beside or beneath the grate where hardwood logs burn and produce embers.
- **Parrilla grate:** The adjustable cooking surface. Grate height is controlled by a wheel, a chain, or by lifting the grate manually. High grate = gentle heat; low grate = intense sear.

The cook never moves the meat to the fire. The cook moves the fire (embers) to be under the meat, and adjusts the grate height to control intensity.

If your parrilla does not have a built-in brasero, build your ember-making fire in a separate steel container or brick enclosure beside the grate, and shovel coals under the grate as needed.

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Buy hardwood \u2014 quebracho, eucalyptus, or oak \u2014 not briquettes.
- Invest in long-handled tools \u2014 short handles are dangerous on a parrilla.
- Keep a shovel specifically for moving coals.
- Use sal gruesa from a wide-mouth container, not a salt shaker.
- Stock more wood than you think you need \u2014 a full asado burns through 10\u201315 lb.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t use lighter fluid \u2014 it taints the early coals.
- Don\u2019t use softwood or treated wood.
- Don\u2019t use thin, flimsy tongs on heavy cuts.
- Don\u2019t forget to clean the grate while still warm after cooking.
