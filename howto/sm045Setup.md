# SM045 Setup & First Cook

A step-by-step guide to seasoning your SM045, understanding how it works, and cooking your first smoke successfully.

---

## How the SM045 works

Understanding the machine makes you a better operator.

The SM045 is an **insulated electric cabinet smoker**. A 750-watt heating element at the bottom cycles on and off to maintain the setpoint temperature. A removable steel wood box sits directly over the element — wood placed inside smolders and produces smoke without an open flame.

Key characteristics:
- **Double-walled stainless steel** with Spin-Glas insulation — it holds heat extremely well
- **Low airflow, high humidity** — the sealed cabinet traps moisture, which keeps meat juicy but slows bark formation
- **Set-and-forget** — once you dial in the temp and load the wood, there is very little to do
- **Temperature range:** 140–300°F in 5-degree increments
- **No fire management** — no charcoal, no vents, no wood splits to add

The biggest difference from an offset or charcoal smoker is that **everything happens in a closed, humid environment**. This is good for moisture retention but requires restraint with wood amounts — smoke concentrates quickly in the enclosed space.

## Step 1 — Season the SM045 before first use

Before your first real cook, run the SM045 empty to burn off any manufacturing oils and condition the interior. This is called a "seasoning run."

1. Remove all racks, the drip pan, and the wood box.
2. Set the controller to **250°F**.
3. Run empty for **2–3 hours** with the door closed.
4. Turn off and let cool completely.
5. Repeat once more at **275°F** for 1 hour.

After two seasoning runs, the interior will have a light brown patina. That's correct. Do not clean the interior with soap — that patina protects the metal and contributes to flavor over time.

---

## Step 2 — Know your controller

The SM045 controller is simple:

| Display | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Upper number | Cabinet air temperature |
| Lower number | Meat probe temperature |
| Up/down buttons | Adjust setpoint in 5°F increments |

**Important:** The controller does not shut off the heat automatically when the meat probe hits a target. You must monitor the probe temp yourself and pull the meat manually.

During the initial heat-up, the cabinet will **briefly overshoot** the setpoint — sometimes by 10–20°F. This is normal. It settles back to setpoint within 20–30 minutes.

---

## Step 3 — Load wood correctly

The wood box is the heart of the SM045 smoke system. Get this right and everything else follows.

**Rules:**
- Use **chunks only** — 1–2" pieces. Not chips (they burn too fast), not pellets (they smother), not logs (too much mass).
- **Weigh your wood.** Use a kitchen scale. "1–2 oz" is a real measurement, not a handful.
- Load wood **at the start of the cook**, before you load meat.
- Do **not** open the door to add more wood mid-cook. The first charge is designed to carry the full cook.

**Why restraint matters:** The SM045 has low airflow. Smoke builds up inside the cabinet instead of flowing through and exiting. More wood in this environment produces harsher, more bitter smoke than you'd get with the same amount on a high-draft offset. The manufacturer baseline is 1–2 oz per 60 lb of food. For home loads (5–7 lb), 1–2 oz total is correct.

## Step 4 — First cook walkthrough (pork butt or ribs recommended)

Pork shoulder (Boston butt) or baby back ribs are ideal first SM045 cooks because they're forgiving and showcase what the machine does well.

### Before you start

- Season and rub your meat the night before.
- Pull from the fridge 30–60 minutes before smoking.

### Preheat the SM045

1. Set controller to **235–250°F**.
2. Close the door and let preheat for **30–60 minutes** before loading meat.
3. Weigh your wood — 2 oz of hickory or apple for pork butt; 1–2 oz for ribs.
4. Place wood in the wood box. Close the box lid.

### Load the meat

1. Place meat on the middle rack. Fat-side up for pork butt.
2. Insert the meat probe into the thickest part, avoiding large fat pockets.
3. Close the SM045 door. Do not reopen it for at least 2 hours.

### During the cook

- Let the controller maintain temperature. You don't need to adjust anything.
- Check the probe display periodically — do not open the door to check.
- For pork butt: expect 6–10 hours to reach 198–205°F probe-tender. The stall (a long temperature plateau around 165–175°F) is normal.
- For ribs: expect 4–6 hours. Judge by the bend test and bone pullback, not temperature.

### Finishing

- Pull the meat when probe-tender, not just when it hits a temperature.
- For pork butt: rest wrapped in butcher paper for 1–2 hours before pulling.
- For ribs: rest 15–30 minutes before slicing.

## Common first-cook mistakes

| Mistake | What happens | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Too much wood | Acrid, bitter smoke flavor | Use 1–2 oz max. Weigh it. |
| Opening door constantly | Lost heat, extended cook time | Trust the probe. Open only when necessary. |
| Expecting offset-style bark | Soft, pale exterior | SM045 bark is softer; plan an unwrapped finish phase |
| Not preheating long enough | Uneven heat at the start | Preheat 30–60 min before loading meat |
| Pulling by time, not tenderness | Undercooked or overcooked | Internal temp + probe tenderness, always |

## Temperature and time quick reference

| Protein | SM045 setpoint | Time window | Pull temp |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Brisket flat (3–5 lb) | 225–250°F | 6–10 hours | 195–205°F probe-tender |
| Pork butt (3–4 lb) | 235–250°F | 6–10 hours | 198–205°F probe-tender |
| Spare ribs (3–4 lb rack) | 225–240°F | 5–7 hours | Bend test + bone pullback |
| Baby back ribs (2–3 lb rack) | 225–240°F | 4–6 hours | Bend test + bone pullback |
| Whole chicken, spatchcocked | 275–300°F | 1.5–2.5 hours | 165°F at thickest point |
| Salmon fillet (1–2 lb) | 200–225°F | 30–60 minutes | 130–140°F |
| Swordfish steaks | 200–225°F | 25–45 minutes | 135–140°F (pull early) |
