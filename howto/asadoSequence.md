A traditional Uruguayan asado is not just a list of things to grill \u2014 it is a carefully ordered sequence built around fire management, social ritual, and the properties of each cut. The sequencing is the tradition. Understanding it turns a cookout into an asado.

***

## La Filosof\u00EDa / The Philosophy

The asado is unhurried by design. Guests do not arrive and immediately eat. They arrive, they have mate or a glass of wine, they watch the fire, they smell the first sausages beginning to cook. The food arrives in waves, each wave marking a progression from lighter to richer, from faster to slower, from thirst-quencher to centrepiece.

The asador\u2019s job is to manage the fire and the sequence simultaneously \u2014 ensuring that each item is ready at the right moment, that the slow cuts get their full time, and that no one waits too long between anything.

***

## Fase 1: Preparaci\u00F3n / Preparation (T-90 to T-60 minutes)

**What happens here:** Light the fire. Prepare the meat. Receive guests with mate or aperitivo.

1. **Light the brasero** with hardwood logs (quebracho, eucalyptus, or oak). A cross-stack of 3\u20134 logs, lit with newspaper.
2. **Season the main cuts** \u2014 asado de tira, vac\u00EDo, bondiola, pamplona. Salt generously with sal gruesa on the side going down first, then set aside.
3. **Prepare the chimichurri** (ideally made the day before; if not, make it now and let it rest).
4. **Prepare the ensalada mixta** \u2014 lettuce torn, tomatoes sliced, onion rings ready. Do not dress yet.
5. **Bury the papas al rescoldo** in the brasero coal bed at T-60 (when coals are established), so they will be ready when the main cuts arrive.
6. **Receive guests** with mate or a glass of medio y medio (Uruguay\u2019s traditional vermouth-and-sparkling-wine aperitivo). This is the sobremesa before the meal \u2014 the time for the fire to settle and the party to gather.

**Fire status at T-60:** The brasero should have a good, active fire. Some coals are forming but the fire is still lively. The grate is not yet in use.

***

## Fase 2: Achuras y Embutidos / Offal and Sausages (T-60 to T-30 minutes)

**What happens here:** The first wave of food arrives at the table. Guests eat while the main cuts begin.

**Items:**
- **Morcilla (blood sausage):** Goes on the edge of the grate over gentle heat. 12\u201318 minutes. Served with bread.
- **Chorizo criollo:** Goes on the mid grate over medium heat. 15\u201320 minutes. Served in bread as choripán.
- **Provoleta (optional):** Cast-iron skillet or directly on the grate, 3\u20136 minutes. Served with crusty bread.
- **Mollejas (sweetbreads, optional):** Goes on the mid-high grate, 20\u201325 minutes. Often the most coveted achura.

**This wave is social.** Guests eat standing up or moving around. There are no seats assigned; everyone is near the parrilla. The choripán and morcilla are passed by hand. Cold beer or young wine is poured.

**Fire status at T-45 to T-30:** The brasero has its first deep coal bed. The asador rakes the first layer of embers under the grate for the achuras and simultaneously prepares to put the long beef cuts on.

***

## Fase 3: Cortes Lentos / Slow Beef Cuts (T-60 to T-0)

**What happens here:** The main beef cuts go on the grate at the same time as or just after the achuras, and cook for 60\u201390 minutes while guests work through the first wave.

**Items:**
- **Asado de tira:** Bone-side down, high grate. 60\u201390 minutes total.
- **Vac\u00EDo:** Fat-side down, mid grate. 45\u201360 minutes total.
- **Bondiola:** High grate, browning all sides first then slow cook. 90\u2013120 minutes total.
- **Pamplona (if serving):** Mid-high grate, rotating every 15\u201320 minutes. 60\u201380 minutes total.

**The asador\u2019s role during this phase:** Tend the brasero. Rake fresh coals every 20\u201330 minutes. Manage grate height. Listen for the sizzle. This phase is the heart of the asado and requires sustained attention \u2014 not constant hands-on work, but consistent awareness.

**The guests\u2019 role during this phase:** Eat the achuras. Drink wine. Talk. Understand that the main event is coming and worth waiting for.

***

## Fase 4: Cortes R\u00E1pidos / Fast Cuts (T-15 to T-0)

**What happens here:** Just before the slow cuts are pulled to rest, the fast cuts go on the hot grate. The sequence overlaps.

**Items:**
- **Entra\u00F1a:** Low grate, hot coals, 6\u20138 minutes total. Goes on last.
- **Matambre:** Mid grate, 12\u201318 minutes total. Goes on 15\u201320 minutes before serving.
- **Morrones asados:** Were grilling earlier on the mid grate; now resting and dressed in olive oil.

**The logic of the overlap:** The slow cuts are pulled to rest for 8\u201310 minutes. During that rest time, the entra\u00F1a or matambre finishes on the now-hot grate. Everything arrives at the table simultaneously.

***

## Fase 5: El Servicio / Service (T-0)

**What happens here:** The whole table sits down together. The main wave of meat is served.

1. Carry the asado de tira, vac\u00EDo, entra\u00F1a, matambre, and bondiola to a central cutting board.
2. Slice and portion at the board, not at the grate.
3. Chimichurri and salsa criolla on the table \u2014 not applied, passed.
4. Dress the ensalada mixta now.
5. Excavate the papas al rescoldo from the brasero and serve split-open with butter.
6. Serve the morrones asados at room temperature from their dressing bowl.

**The asador sits down now.** This is important. The work is done. The asador is a guest at their own meal. This is the tradition.

***

## La Sobremesa / After the Meal

**Sobremesa** \u2014 literally \u201cover the table\u201d \u2014 is the long, unhurried conversation that follows the meal. Plates are cleared, but no one leaves. More wine is poured. Uruguayan flan with dulce de leche arrives at some point. Mate circulates again. The conversation continues for an hour or two.

The asado without sobremesa is merely grilling. The sobremesa is what makes it an asado.

***

## Resumen / Sequence at a Glance

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| T-90 | Light brasero. |
| T-60 | Bury papas al rescoldo. Season main cuts. |
| T-60 to T-45 | First coal bed ready. Put morcilla and chorizo on grate. |
| T-50 | Put asado de tira and vac\u00EDo on grate. |
| T-45 | Serve morcilla and choripán to guests. First wave. |
| T-30 | Put bondiola on grate (if serving). |
| T-20 | Put matambre on grate. |
| T-10 | Pull slow cuts to rest. Put entra\u00F1a on grate. |
| T-0 | Everything sliced and served. Asador sits down. |
| T+30 | Flan and dulce de leche. Sobremesa begins. |

***

## S\u00ED hac\u00E9 / Do\u2019s

- Start the fire 60\u201390 minutes before the first guest arrives.
- Begin with achuras \u2014 they buy time and set the social tone.
- Put slow cuts on early; put fast cuts on last.
- Maintain the brasero throughout the entire cook.
- Sit down when the main wave is served \u2014 the asador is also a guest.

## No hag\u00E1s / Don\u2019ts

- Don\u2019t serve everything at once \u2014 the sequence is the experience.
- Don\u2019t rush the slow cuts with high heat.
- Don\u2019t let the brasero die in the middle of the cook.
- Don\u2019t apply chimichurri during cooking \u2014 always at the table.
- Don\u2019t skip the sobremesa.
